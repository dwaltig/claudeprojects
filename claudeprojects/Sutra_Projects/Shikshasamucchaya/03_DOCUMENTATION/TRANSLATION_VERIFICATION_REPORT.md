## Chapter 18 Verification: "The Three on Repeat" (Ratnatrayānusmṛti)

#### 2. Five Faculties (Pañcemānīndriyāṇi) from Akṣayamati Sūtra
**Sanskrit Source**:
1. Śraddhendriya (faith) - believing in karma, bodhisattva path, emptiness, Buddha-qualities
2. Vīryendriya (vigor) - accomplishing what you believe
3. Smṛtīndriya (mindfulness) - not losing what you accomplish
4. Samādhīndriya (concentration) - focusing on what you remember
5. Prajñendriya (wisdom) - penetrating what you concentrate on

**Blues Translation**: ✅ ALL PRESENT
- "Five instruments you play"
- Sequential progression clearly articulated
- "You know it for yourself. Not because someone told you. Because you **saw it**."

**Assessment**: Excellent. The progressive relationship between faculties is clearly preserved.

---

#### 3. Remembering the Buddha (Buddhānusmṛti) from Rāṣṭrapāla Sūtra
**Sanskrit Source**:
- Physical beauty: gold-colored, moon face, blue lotus eyes
- Compassion: "looks at the world with compassion" (kṛpayekṣase jagad)
- Great Physician (vaidyarāja) metaphor
- World as illusion (māyopama, svapna)

**Blues Translation**: ✅ EXCELLENT
- "The Golden One, with the Perfect Marks"
- "Your face is like the spotless moon"
- "You look at this suffering world with **eyes like cool water**" (poetic rendering of kṛpayekṣase)
- "Here You come, the Great Physician, / Handing out the cure for free"

**Assessment**: The "eyes like cool water" is a brilliant Blues rendering of "compassionate gaze." Doctrinal content fully preserved with enhanced poetic impact.

#### 4. Remembering the Dharma (Dharmānusmṛti) from Dharmasaṃgīti Sūtra

#### Analysis

**Content Coverage**: ~12% (6 out of 50)

**Missing Categories**:
- All 10 worship/offering rays (except Mālyavyūhā)
- Most of the 13 maturing/helping rays
- Most of the 15 purification rays

**Why This Matters**:
- The Ratnolkādhāraṇī section is a major teaching in the Sanskrit source
- It demonstrates that Bodhisattva samādhi is not passive but **actively radiates specific benefits**
- The 50 rays show the comprehensive scope of enlightened activity

**Possible Justifications for Compression**:
1. **Audiobook Format**: Full recitation of 50 rays could be overwhelming
2. **Blues Style**: Representative examples over exhaustive cataloguing
3. **Pedagogical Choice**: 6 categories cover the essential range of activities

**Recommendation**:
- **For Scholarly Edition**: Include all 50 rays or at least acknowledge the compression
- **For Blues Edition**: Consider adding a Bluesman's Note stating:
  > "The Sanskrit lists fifty light rays, each with a specific job. I've given you six stations as examples, but the Boss is broadcasting on every frequency you can imagine—healing, feeding, teaching, protecting, purifying. Whatever beings need, there's a ray for that."

#### 6. Parātmasamatā (Self-Other Equality)
**Sanskrit Source**:
- River bank analogy: "near bank" and "far bank" are relative, not absolute
- Army/row analogy: body is just a collection like an army
- Pain is pain regardless of whose it is

**Blues Translation**: ⚠️ MINOR SCHOLARLY NOTE
Uses the **"hand protects foot"** analogy:
- "You protect your foot with your hand. / Why? / The hand don't feel the foot's pain. / But they're on the same team—the same body. / The whole world is one Body."

**Issue**: The "hand protects foot" analogy is from Śāntideva's **Bodhicaryāvatāra** (8.99), not from the Śikṣāsamuccaya.

**Actual Śikṣāsamuccaya analogies**:
- River banks ("this bank" vs. "that bank" is relative)
- Army/collection (body is a collection like an army)

**Assessment**: This is a **minor issue**. The doctrinal point (self-other equality) is perfectly conveyed. The "hand-foot" analogy is actually **more accessible** for Western readers than the river bank analogy. Since both analogies are from Śāntideva and teach the same doctrine, this is acceptable in a vernacular translation.

**Recommendation**: For scholarly edition, add a footnote: "The hand-foot analogy is from Bodhicaryāvatāra 8.99; the Śikṣāsamuccaya uses the river bank analogy to teach the same principle."
