## GLOSSARY OF BUDDHIST TERMS

**Afflictions** (kleśa)
Mental states that cloud the mind and perpetuate suffering, including greed, hatred, delusion, pride, doubt, and wrong views. Described as needing to be washed away like dirt.

**Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya** (Sanskrit: आज्ञात-कौण्डिन्य)
One of the first five disciples of the Buddha who heard the teaching of the Four Noble Truths at Deer Park in Bārāṇasī (Varanasi). His name means "he who has understood."

**Ānanda** (Sanskrit: आनन्द)
The Buddha's cousin and personal attendant for twenty-five years, renowned for his exceptional memory of the Buddha's teachings. His name means "bliss" or "joy."

**Anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi** (Sanskrit: अनुत्तर-सम्यक्-संबोधि)
Supreme perfect enlightenment; the complete awakening of a Buddha. Often translated as "unsurpassed perfect awakening." The highest spiritual attainment.

**Arhat** (Sanskrit: अर्हत्)
"Worthy one"; a person who has attained the highest level of realization in early Buddhism by eliminating all defilements and will not be reborn. The fourth and final fruit of the voice-hearer path.

**Asura** (Sanskrit: असुर)
A class of powerful but jealous supernatural beings, often in conflict with the gods. One of the six realms of existence and one of the eight classes of beings who protect the Dharma.

**Avalokiteśvara** (Sanskrit: अवलोकितेश्वर)
"The Lord Who Looks Down with Compassion"; the great bodhisattva of compassion who hears the cries of beings and responds to their suffering. Also known as Guanyin in Chinese tradition.

**Avataṃsaka Sutra**
The "Flower Garland Sutra"; one of the most influential Mahayana sutras, describing the enlightened perspective of the Buddha and the bodhisattva path through innumerable stages.

**Bārāṇasī** (Sanskrit: वाराणसी)
Ancient city in northern India (modern Varanasi) where the Buddha first taught the Four Noble Truths to his first five disciples at Deer Park.

**Bodhi**
Awakening or enlightenment; the direct realization of ultimate reality that liberates one from cyclic existence.

**Bodhisattva** (Sanskrit: बोधिसत्त्व)
"Enlightenment being"; one who seeks awakening not only for themselves but dedicates their practice to liberating all sentient beings. A practitioner of the Great Vehicle (Mahāyāna) path.

**Brahma-voice**
The pure, resonant voice of the Buddha, one of his extraordinary qualities. Said to have eight characteristics including clarity, depth, and the ability to reach all listeners appropriately.

**Buddha** (Sanskrit: बुद्ध)
"Awakened One"; one who has achieved complete enlightenment and liberation from cyclic existence, possessing perfect wisdom and compassion.

**Dharma** (Sanskrit: धर्म)
Multiple meanings: (1) The teachings of the Buddha; (2) phenomena or things; (3) truth or ultimate reality; (4) the way things are; (5) righteousness or duty.

**Dhāraṇī** (Sanskrit: धारणी)
"Retaining" or "sustaining"; mystical formulas or concentrated teachings that contain and convey vast meaning. Also refers to the power of memory and retention.

**Dharmāgatayaśas**
The Central Indian monk and Tripiṭaka Master who translated this sutra into Chinese. He resided at Chaoting Temple in Guangzhou and knew both Chinese characters and spoken Chinese.

**Dharma-body** (dharmakāya)
The ultimate nature of Buddha; the truth-body that transcends all form and limitation, representing the Buddha's realization of ultimate reality itself.

**Dharma-door**
A teaching, practice, or method that provides entry into understanding and realizing the Dharma.

**Dharma-wheel**
The teaching of the Buddha; to "turn the Dharma-wheel" means to teach the Dharma. Refers to the Buddha setting the wheel of truth in motion.

**Five Precepts**
The basic ethical guidelines for Buddhist laypeople: refraining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech, and intoxicants.

**Four Noble Truths**
The Buddha's fundamental teaching: (1) the truth of suffering; (2) the truth of the origin of suffering; (3) the truth of the cessation of suffering; (4) the truth of the path leading to cessation.

**Gandharva** (Sanskrit: गन्धर्व)
Celestial musicians who serve the gods; one of the eight classes of supernatural beings who protect the Dharma.

**Garuḍa** (Sanskrit: गरुड)
Mythical bird beings of enormous size and power; one of the eight classes of beings who protect the Dharma.

**Great Vehicle** (Mahāyāna)
The bodhisattva path emphasizing the cultivation of compassion and wisdom to liberate all beings, not just oneself.

**Jambudvīpa** (Sanskrit: जम्बुद्वीप)
"Rose-apple continent"; the southern continent in Buddhist cosmology, representing the human world and where the Buddha's teachings flourish.

**Jian Yuan period**
A reign period of the Qi dynasty in China (479-482 CE).

**Kinnara** (Sanskrit: किन्नर)
Celestial musicians with human bodies and horse heads; one of the eight classes of beings who protect the Dharma.

**Mahākāśyapa** (Sanskrit: महाकाश्यप)
One of the Buddha's principal disciples, renowned for his ascetic practices and chosen to convene the First Buddhist Council after the Buddha's passing.

**Mahāprajñāpāramitā**
The great perfection of wisdom teachings; extensive sutras on the nature of emptiness and non-duality.

**Mahāsthāmaprāpta** (Sanskrit: महास्थामप्राप्त)
"He Who Has Attained Great Strength"; a great bodhisattva who, along with Avalokiteśvara, attends Amitābha Buddha.

**Mahoraga** (Sanskrit: महोरग)
Great serpent deities; one of the eight classes of beings who protect the Dharma.

**Maitreya** (Sanskrit: मैत्रेय)
The future Buddha who currently resides in the Tuṣita Heaven and will appear in the world when the teachings of Śākyamuni Buddha have been forgotten.

**Mañjuśrī** (Sanskrit: मञ्जुश्री)
The bodhisattva of wisdom, often depicted holding a sword that cuts through ignorance and a text representing the perfection of wisdom.

**Maudgalyāyana** (Sanskrit: मौद्गल्यायन)
One of the Buddha's two chief disciples (with Śāriputra), renowned for his supernatural powers.

**Nayuta**
A very large number in Indian counting, variously estimated as between 100,000 and 10 million.

**Nirvāṇa** (Sanskrit: निर्वाण)
The state of liberation from cyclic existence; the extinction of craving, aversion, and delusion; the unconditioned peace beyond birth and death.

**Non-arising** (anutpāda)
The realization that phenomena do not ultimately arise or cease; insight into the empty nature of all things.

**One Vehicle** (ekayāna)
The teaching that all three vehicles (voice-hearer, solitary realizer, and bodhisattva) ultimately lead to the same enlightenment; there is fundamentally only one path to Buddhahood.

**Parables Chapter**
A chapter from the Lotus Sutra using metaphors to illustrate the teaching of the One Vehicle.

**Patient acceptance of the non-arising of phenomena** (anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti)
A high bodhisattva attainment involving deep realization that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence and do not ultimately arise or cease.

**Qi dynasty** (Xiao Qi)
The Southern Qi dynasty in China (479-502 CE), during which this sutra was translated.

**Rāhula** (Sanskrit: राहुल)
The Buddha's son, born before his renunciation. Later became one of his disciples, known for keeping precepts strictly.

**Rājagṛha** (Sanskrit: राजगृह)
"Royal residence"; ancient capital of Magadha in India where many important Buddhist teachings were given.

**Samādhi** (Sanskrit: समाधि)
Meditative absorption; concentrated states of deep mental stability and clarity achieved through meditation practice.

**Śāriputra** (Sanskrit: शारिपुत्र)
One of the Buddha's two chief disciples (with Maudgalyāyana), foremost in wisdom. Key figure in Mahayana sutras representing the voice-hearer perspective.

**Seven stages**
The progressive teaching methods of the Buddha: (1) Five Precepts; (2) Four Noble Truths; (3) Twelve Links; (4) Six Perfections; (5) Immeasurable Meanings; (6) Lotus Sutra; (7) eternal Buddha-nature teaching.

**Skillful means** (upāya)
Provisional or expedient teachings adapted to the capacities of different beings to guide them toward ultimate truth.

**Solitary Buddha** (pratyekabuddha)
One who attains enlightenment through contemplating dependent origination without a teacher in that lifetime, but does not teach widely.

**Śrāvaka** (Sanskrit: श्रावक)
"Voice-hearer" or "disciple"; practitioners who achieve liberation primarily through hearing and following the Buddha's teachings on the Four Noble Truths.

**Stream-entry**
The first of four stages of awakening in early Buddhism, ensuring one will not be reborn in lower realms and will attain full liberation within seven lifetimes at most.

**Subhūti** (Sanskrit: सुभूति)
One of the Buddha's principal disciples, foremost in understanding emptiness and dwelling in peace.

**Tathāgata** (Sanskrit: तथागत)
"Thus-gone" or "Thus-come"; an epithet of the Buddha referring to one who has realized ultimate reality and teaches it to others.

**Three Realms**
The desire realm, form realm, and formless realm—the totality of cyclic existence in Buddhist cosmology.

**Three times**
Past, present, and future.

**Three Vehicles**
The paths of the voice-hearer (śrāvaka), solitary realizer (pratyekabuddha), and bodhisattva, later revealed in Mahayana to be expedient divisions of one ultimate vehicle.

**Tripiṭaka Master**
"Master of the Three Baskets"; a monk who has mastered the three divisions of the Buddhist canon: Sutra (discourses), Vinaya (discipline), and Abhidharma (higher teachings).

**Tuṣita Heaven**
The "Joyous Heaven"; the fourth of six desire realm heavens where Maitreya currently resides before descending to become the next Buddha.

**Twelve Links of Dependent Origination** (pratītyasamutpāda)
The teaching of the chain of causation explaining how suffering arises: ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and old age and death.

**Twin Trees**
The sala trees between which the Buddha lay down and entered final nirvana at Kuśinagara.

**Upāli** (Sanskrit: उपालि)
One of the Buddha's principal disciples, foremost in keeping the monastic discipline (vinaya).

**Vaipulya scriptures**
"Extensive" Mahayana sutras; expansive teachings on the bodhisattva path.

**Vimalakīrti** (Sanskrit: विमलकीर्ति)
"He Whose Fame is Stainless"; a wealthy layman bodhisattva who was the central figure in the Vimalakīrti Sutra, renowned for profound wisdom.

**Voice-hearer** (śrāvaka)
See Śrāvaka above.

**Vulture Peak** (Gṛdhrakūṭa)
A mountain near Rājagṛha where the Buddha taught many important Mahayana sutras, including the Lotus Sutra and this Immeasurable Meanings Sutra.

**Wheel-turning king** (cakravartin)
A righteous universal monarch who rules through Dharma rather than force, possessing supernatural wheels of different metals according to his domain.

**World-Honored One** (Bhagavān)
An epithet of the Buddha, one honored and revered throughout the world.

**Yakṣa** (Sanskrit: यक्ष)
Nature spirits or guardian deities; one of the eight classes of beings who protect the Dharma.

**Yong Ming period**
A reign period of the Southern Qi dynasty in China (483-493 CE).

## REFERENCES AND BIBLIOGRAPHY

### Primary Source Text

**Title:** Sutra of Immeasurable Meanings (無量義經, *Wuliang Yi Jing*)
**Sanskrit reconstruction:** *Amitārtha Sūtra* or *Ananta Nirdeśa Pratiharya Sūtra*
**Chinese translation:** Attributed to Dharmāgatayaśas (曇摩伽陀耶舍), Tripiṭaka Master from Central India
**Translation period:** Xiao Qi Dynasty (Southern Qi, 479-502 CE), specifically cited as Year 3 of Yong Ming period (485 CE) in the preface
**Original translator location:** Chaoting Temple (朝亭寺), Guangzhou, China
**Transmission:** Brought to Mount Wudang by monk Huibiao (慧表) on the 18th day of the 9th month, Year 3 of Yong Ming (485 CE)

### Blues Edition Translation

**Translator/Adapter:** [To be specified by author]
**Translation date:** [To be specified]
**Base text:** Chinese Taishō Tripiṭaka edition (T.276)
**Style:** Blues/gospel vernacular English adaptation

### Historical Context (from Preface)

**Preface author:** Liu Qiu (劉虬), hermit of Jingzhou
**Preface composition:** Xiao Qi Dynasty period (Southern Qi, 479-502 CE)
**Historical figures mentioned:**
- Huibiao (慧表): Monk of Qiang ethnicity, nephew of Yao Lue
- Yao Lue (姚略): Pretender emperor whose kingdom fell to Jin forces
- He Danzhi (何丹之): Jin general who raised Huibiao
- Dharmāgatayaśas: Indian monk and translator who knew Chinese

**Related dynasty periods cited:**
- Jian Yuan period (建元): Years 1-4 (479-482 CE)
- Yong Ming period (永明): Years 1-11 (483-493 CE)

### Canonical Position

**Relationship to Lotus Sutra:** Described as the "opening sutra" or preparatory teaching immediately preceding the Lotus Sutra in the teaching sequence. Mentioned in the Lotus Sutra's opening chapter but historically unavailable in China until this translation.

**Position in doctrinal development:** Stage 5 of 7 in the preface's schema of progressive teachings:
1. Five Precepts (for humans/gods)
2. Four Noble Truths (for voice-hearers)
3. Twelve Links (for solitary realizers)
4. Six Perfections (for bodhisattvas/Great Vehicle)
5. **Immeasurable Meanings Sutra** (transitional teaching)
6. Lotus Sutra (One Vehicle teaching)
7. Nirvana Sutra (eternal Buddha-nature teaching)

### Doctrinal Schools Referenced (Preface)

**Chinese Buddhist Masters:**
- Master Zhi (支): Teaching on no-arising (無生)
- Master An (安): Teaching on different contemplations (異觀)
- Master Sengzhao (僧肇): Author of treatises on emptiness and sudden enlightenment
- References to gradual vs. sudden enlightenment debates in early Chinese Buddhism

### Philosophical Frameworks

**Dependent Origination (pratītyasamutpāda):** Twelve Links as middle teaching
**Emptiness (śūnyatā):** Central theme throughout, especially in preface philosophical discussion
**Buddha-nature:** Eternal self teaching reserved for final teaching (Stage 7)
**Skillful means (upāya):** Progressive pedagogy adapting to capacities
**Non-duality:** Formless yet manifesting form; one taste in many teachings

### Liturgical and Cultural Context

**Ritual elements described:**
- Circumambulation (pradakṣiṇa): 100,000 times
- Prostration: heads touching Buddha's feet
- Offerings: incense, flowers, robes, jeweled necklaces, food, music, banners
- Verse praise (gāthā): Extended verses of glorification
- Signs and wonders: Earth-shaking, heavenly rain of offerings

**Audience composition:**
- 80,000 great bodhisattvas (named leaders)
- 12,000 arhat monks (named leaders)
- Fourfold assembly: monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen
- Eight classes of beings: gods, dragons, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kinnaras, mahoragas
- Royalty: wheel-turning kings, regional kings, princes, ministers

### Contemporary Scholarly Resources

**Standard translations and studies:**
- Bunno Kato, et al. *The Threefold Lotus Sutra* (1975) - English translation
- Senchu Murano, *The Sutra of Infinite Meanings* (1974)
- Jan Nattier, *A Few Good Men: The Bodhisattva Path according to The Inquiry of Ugra* (2003) - discusses early Mahayana context

**Critical studies:**
- Discussions of authenticity: Debated whether originally Indian or Chinese composition
- Stylistic analysis: Vocabulary and concepts align with late 5th-century Chinese Buddhism
- Historical transmission: Questions about whether Dharmāgatayaśas translation or Chinese composition

### Manuscript and Textual History

**Chinese recensions:** Considered a single translation tradition
**Translation status:** No known Sanskrit original; possibly composed in Chinese or translated from lost Sanskrit text
**Commentarial tradition:** Received commentaries from major Chinese Buddhist schools, especially Tiantai school, which used it as opening text for Lotus Sutra study
