  The Core Methodological Shift

  The article challenges a foundational assumption in Africana religious
  studies: that Black religious traditions have discrete genealogies (Africa
   → diaspora; Christianity → Black church; folk traditions → contemporary
  practice).

  Instead, it argues: Black religious creativity has always been a global 
  synthesis. Black communities don't just receive traditions; they absorb,
  remix, and repurpose spiritual technologies from wherever they find them
  effective.

  ---
  Five Ways This Advances the Field

  1. Reframes "Appropriation" as "Survival"

  Historically, Africana studies has been protective (rightly) about
  cultural theft. The binary was:
  - Bad: White appropriation of Black culture
  - Good: Authentic Black tradition

  This article says: that's too simple. The "zombie text" framework lets
  scholars ask: What in this cross-cultural encounter cannot be extracted? 
  What must be survived?

  For JAR readers, this opens up: How are contemporary Black communities
  drawing on global spiritual resources? What ethical frameworks should
  guide that work?

  ---
  2. Theorizes the Black Buddhist Turn as Africana Religion

  This is significant. Historically, Buddhism wasn't "Africana religion."
  But now there's a real phenomenon: Black Buddhist practitioners,
  scholarship by Black Buddhist thinkers (Majied, Vesely-Flad, Manuel).

  The article says: This is an important development in how Black peoples 
  are creating meaning. It's not "borrowing from Asia"; it's the Black
  radical tradition doing what it's always done.

  For the field: This legitimizes Buddhist studies as part of Africana
  religious studies—not as appropriation, but as evidence of Black
  intellectual creativity.

  ---
  3. Provides New Philosophical Language for Black Freedom

  The concept of "Emptiness as Mobility" is powerful because it gives
  scholars a rigorous vocabulary for something Black communities have always
   known:

  - The system tries to fix you in place ("Negro," "convict," "welfare
  recipient," "threat")
  - Freedom isn't building a solid counter-identity; it's refusing solidity
  itself
  - This isn't nihilism; it's hypermobility—the refusal to be pinned down

  For JAR: This could reshape how we theorize Black autonomy, radical
  refusal, and freedom. Instead of "resistance," we could talk about
  "ontological defiance."

  ---
  4. Centers Black Scholars as Primary Theorists

  By making Majied, Vesely-Flad, Manuel the main voices (not supporting
  players), the article insists: The Black Buddhist Turn is being theorized 
  by Black thinkers.

  This is methodologically important because it respects Black intellectual
  authority on Black religion—which is both obvious and historically rare in
   academia.

  For JAR: It models a practice of centering Black scholarship rather than
  tokenizing it.

  ---
  5. Engages Digital/Contemporary Concerns

  The "digital minstrelsy" and "zombie text" framework addresses real
  contemporary issues:
  - How do we ethically produce scholarship in an AI age?
  - What gets extracted when we digitize culture?

  For JAR: This brings Africana religious studies into conversation with
  digital humanities, AI ethics, and contemporary cultural production.
