  First, we move beyond models of Black religion that trace influence solely through Atlantic genealogies. The Black Buddhist Turn—currently emerging in the work of Majied, Vesely-Flad, Manuel, and others—suggests that Black communities have always drawn on global resources to forge survival technologies. This isn't "borrowing"; it's the same creative synthesis that produced the Blues itself from African polyrhythms, Protestant hymns, and work songs.

  Second, it invites comparative work grounded in structural resonance rather than historical genealogy. Future scholarship might examine the resonances between Madhyamaka and the apophatic traditions of enslaved Muslims, or between tantric visualization and the "conjure" traditions of the Black South—not because there was direct transmission, but because similar crises generate similar epistemological responses.

  Third, and most critically, it insists that this work must be theorized by Black scholars. My role here has been to map a resonance and to stay accountable to the limits of my position. The real work—the work of articulating what the Black Buddhist Turn means for Black communities—belongs to the scholars who are living it.
