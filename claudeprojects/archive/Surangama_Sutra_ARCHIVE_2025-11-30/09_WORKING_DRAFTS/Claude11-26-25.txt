### The Citation
* **Author:** James H. Cone
* **Book:** *God of the Oppressed*
* **Chapter:** 9 ("God and Black Ethics")

### The Quote

> "The black slave experience did not accept the white view of truth... **Deception was a way of survival.** ... To

Moses" who embodied this theology of action, stating:
> *"Harriet Tubman, the 'Black Moses,' who led hundreds of slaves to freedom... represents the black religious 
experience."*

Cone integrates the "by any means necessary" ethic (derived from Malcolm X) into Christian theology by arguing that 
God sides with the oppressed, validating their methods of survival (including the deception of "Pharaoh").

       62      Consider a specific example. The Lotus Sutra contains the parable of the Burning House,[16] 
            perhaps the most famous teaching in the entire text. In Kumarajiva's Classical Chinese translation, 
            the parable describes a father who uses "expedient means" (upāya) to save his children from a house 
            on fire. He offers them the promise of different carts (pulled by sheep, deer, and oxen) to entice 
            them out, knowing that he actually has only one great cart for them all. The Buddhist meaning is that
             the Buddha uses different teachings to meet beings where they are, ultimately leading them to the 
            one supreme teaching.
       63      Here is how the opening appears in the formal English translation, preserving Kumarajiva's 
            structure: "Śāriputra, suppose in a certain land, province, or village there was a great elder who 
            was aged and declining, possessing immeasurable wealth, with many fields, estates, and servants. His 
            house was vast and great, having only a single gate. A great many people--one, two, or even five 
            hundred--were dwelling within it. The halls and pavilions were decayed and old, the walls crumbling 
            down, the pillar bases rotten and decomposed, the beams and ridgepoles tilting precariously. All 
            around, simultaneously, fire suddenly broke out, engulfing the dwelling."[28]
       64      Now compare this to the blues rendering: "Śāriputra, say there's a country, a town, a village

             one door. Whole lot of people living in that house--a hundred, two hundred, maybe up to five hundred

> This quote is a summary of the opening argument in **Chapter II: Right View**.

While the exact phrasing *"in all its dimensions"* is often used in summaries of this text to describe the three 
types of *dukkha* (suffering), the specific sentence corresponds to Bhikkhu Bodhi’s definition of **Right View** as 
the necessary starting point of the path.

* **Page Number:** **14** (Standard BPS Pariyatti Edition, 1994/1999/2010)
* **Chapter:** II ("Right View")
* **Context:** The chapter begins by stating that Right View is the "forerunner" of the entire path. Bodhi explains 
that to practice the path, one must first possess "Right View," which he defines specifically as the **conceptual 
understanding of the Four Noble Truths** (the first of which is the Truth of Suffering).

**The exact text on Page 14 reads:**
> "Right view is the forerunner of the entire path... It provides the perspective for the practice... Right view is 
the understanding of the Four Noble Truths."

Later in the chapter (approx. **Page 16–18**), he details what it means to understand suffering "in all its 
dimensions" by breaking it down into:
1.  *Dukkha-dukkha* (pain)
2.  *Viparinama-dukkha* (change)
3.  *Sankhara-dukkha* (conditioned existence)

If you are using this for a citation, it is best to cite **Page 14** for the "Right View as the beginning" concept, 
and **Pages 16–18** for the "dimensions of suffering" analysis.
