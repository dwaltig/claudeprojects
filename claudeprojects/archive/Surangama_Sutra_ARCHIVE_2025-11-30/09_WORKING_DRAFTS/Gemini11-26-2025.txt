> What does the Kalama Sutta say  that parallels?

âœ¦ The Kalama Sutta (Anguttara Nikaya 3.65) contains a specific passage that perfectly parallels your argument about
  "lived experience vs. external authority."

  In the text, the Buddha visits a town where the people (the Kalamas) are confused because many wandering teachers come
  through, each claiming their own doctrine is true and others are false. They ask the Buddha: "Who should we believe?"

  The Buddha's response is the parallel. He lists ten sources of knowledge to reject if they are the only basis for
  belief.

  The Parallel: Rejection of Abstract Authority

  The Buddha says:
  > "Do not go by oral tradition, by lineage of teaching, by hearsay, by a collection of scriptures, by logical
  reasoning, by inferential reasoning, by reasoned cogitation, by the acceptance of a view after pondering it, by the
  seeming competence of a speaker, or because you think: 'The ascetic is our teacher.'"

  How this parallels the Blues:
  This is identical to the Blues' rejection of "abstract" or "high" knowledge. The Blues musician does not sing about
  heartbreak because a book says it hurts; they sing it because they know it. The rejection of "scripture"
  (pitaka-sampadan) and "teacher's authority" (samano no garu) mirrors the Blues' skepticism of "Sunday morning"
  platitudes that don't match "Saturday night" reality.

  The Parallel: The "Validity" Test
