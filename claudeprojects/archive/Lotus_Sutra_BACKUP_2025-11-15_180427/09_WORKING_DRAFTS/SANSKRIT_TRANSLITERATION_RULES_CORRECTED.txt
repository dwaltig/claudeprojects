DETAILED EXPLANATIONS:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. ś → sh (CORRECTED - This was the error)
   ─────────────────────────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: The palatal sibilant ś is pronounced exactly like
   English "sh" in "ship" or "shine". This is NOT the same as regular 's'.

   WHY "sh" NOT "s":
   - The ś sound is produced with the tongue against the palate (roof of mouth)
   - This creates a "sh" sound, not an "s" sound
   - Standard English transliteration uses "sh" for ś
   - Academic convention (IAST, Harvard-Kyoto) represents ś as "sh" when
     converting to ASCII-only text

   EXAMPLES OF CORRECTION:
   - Śāriputra → Shariputra (NOT Sariputra)
   - Śākyamuni → Shakyamuni (already correct in previous version)
   - śrāvaka → shravaka (NOT sravaka)
   - Mañjuśrī → Manjushri (the final ś was correct)
   - Mahākāśyapa → Mahakashyapa (NOT Mahakasyapa)
   - Avalokiteśvara → Avalokiteshvara (already correct)

   PRONUNCIATION VERIFICATION:
   - Shariputra: SHAH-ree-poo-truh ✓ (correct)
   - Sariputra: SAR-ee-poo-truh ✗ (wrong - would be सरिपुत्र not शारिपुत्र)

2. ṇ → n (Retroflex n)
   ──────────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: The retroflex ṇ is pronounced with the tongue
   curled back, touching the roof of the mouth behind the teeth.

   WHY "n" NOT "ny" OR OTHER:
   - English has no distinction between dental n and retroflex ṇ
   - Both are rendered as "n" in English transliteration
   - The difference is negligible to English speakers
   - Standard convention is simple "n"

   EXAMPLES:
   - Pūrṇa → Purna
   - Kauṇḍinya → Kaundinya

   VERDICT: ✓ CORRECT (no change needed)

3. ṃ → m (Anusvara)
   ──────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: The anusvara ṃ is a nasal sound that can be
   pronounced as "m", "n", or "ng" depending on what follows it.

   WHY "m" (or sometimes left silent):
   - In English, it's typically rendered as "m"
   - Sometimes it represents nasalization of the preceding vowel
   - Standard transliteration uses "m"

   EXAMPLES:
   - saṃsāra → samsara
   - saṃgha → sangha (sometimes "samgha")

   VERDICT: ✓ CORRECT (standard convention)

4. ñ → n (Palatal n)
   ────────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: The palatal ñ is pronounced like Spanish "ñ"
   (as in "señor") or like "ny" in "canyon".

   WHY "n" NOT "ny":
   - Standard English transliteration uses simple "n"
   - While "ny" would be more phonetically accurate, it's not conventional
   - Academic systems (IAST) use "ñ" in Unicode, but "n" in ASCII
   - Consistency with established transliterations

   EXAMPLES:
   - Mañjuśrī → Manjushri (NOT Manyushri)
   - Ājñāta → Ajnata (NOT Ajnyata)

   NOTE: Some systems do use "ny" (e.g., some Japanese romanizations), but
   for Sanskrit-to-English, "n" is standard.

   VERDICT: ✓ CORRECT (standard convention)

5. ṭ, ḍ, ḷ → t, d, l (Retroflex consonants)
   ─────────────────────────────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: These are pronounced with tongue curled back,
   touching the roof of the mouth.

   WHY SIMPLE LETTERS:
   - English doesn't distinguish dental vs. retroflex consonants
   - Standard transliteration simplifies to regular letters
   - The difference is negligible to English speakers

   VERDICT: ✓ CORRECT (standard convention)

6. ḥ → h (Visarga)
   ─────────────────
   PHONETIC DESCRIPTION: A voiceless aspiration sound after a vowel.

   WHY "h":
   - Approximates the sound in English
   - Standard transliteration

   VERDICT: ✓ CORRECT (standard convention)

PRONUNCIATION GUIDE FOR VOICE ACTORS
================================================================================

CRITICAL: The corrected transliteration now properly represents pronunciation.

VOWEL SOUNDS:
─────────────
a = "ah" as in "father" (NOT "ay" as in "day")
i = "ee" as in "machine"
u = "oo" as in "rude"
e = "ay" as in "they"
o = "oh" as in "go"

CONSONANT COMBINATIONS:
────────────────────────
sh = "sh" as in "ship" (this is the critical correction)
kh = "k" + breath (aspirated k)
gh = "g" + breath (aspirated g)
ch = "ch" as in "church"
jh = "j" + breath (aspirated j)
th = "t" + breath (NOT "th" as in "the")
dh = "d" + breath (aspirated d)
ph = "p" + breath (NOT "f")
bh = "b" + breath (aspirated b)

STRESS PATTERNS:
────────────────
Most Sanskrit names: stress on SECOND syllable
- sha-REE-pu-tra
- mun-JOO-shree
- uh-vuh-loh-kee-TESH-vuh-ruh

Final "a": Usually pronounced as unstressed "uh"
- Shariputra = Shah-ree-POO-truh (not Shah-ree-POO-trah)
