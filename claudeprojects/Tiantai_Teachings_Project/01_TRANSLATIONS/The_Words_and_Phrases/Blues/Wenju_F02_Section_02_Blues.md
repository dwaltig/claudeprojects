# Fascicle 2, Section 2: Stillness and Eloquence

## Vakkula and Mahākauṣṭhila — The Silent and the Speaker

**Transformed by Master John "The Bluesman" Kim (The Liner Notes Variant)**
**Date**: February 6, 2026
**Root Metaphor**: THE RECORDING STUDIO
**Source**: T34n1718, Fascicle 2

---

Now listen.

These two Arhats are opposites. One never said a word. The other couldn't lose a debate. But both paths lead to the same place — the stillness that speaks, and the speech that goes silent.

---

## Vakkula (薄拘羅): Good Countenance — The Unkillable

### The Name

> "Vakkula (薄拘羅): Translated as 'Good Countenance,' or 'Great Form,' or 'Great Fatness.' Because his physical appearance was upright and correct, it is called 'Good Countenance.'"

### The Biography: Five Attempts on His Life

Vakkula's stepmother tried to kill him five times. Every single attempt failed:

1. **The roasting pan** — he survived
2. **The cauldron of boiling water** — he survived
3. **Thrown into the water** — he survived
4. **Eaten by a fish** — the fish was caught, and he survived inside it
5. **Cut by the knife** (that opened the fish) — he survived

Why? Because in past lives, he kept the precept against killing perfectly. For ninety-one kalpas, his life was never cut short.

He also gave one *haritaki* fruit (a medicinal herb) to the Saṅgha. Result: his body was constantly without sickness.

### The Quietest Monk

> "His body delighted in stillness; he constantly dwelt in secluded leisure, not delighting in crowds. His eyes did not delight in colors; his ears did not delight in sounds; his nose did not smell fragrances; his tongue never once spoke even one or two sentences for other people; his mind was constantly in samādhi."

This is not exaggeration. **He never spoke.** The passage says his tongue "never once spoke one or two sentences."

Even his *stupa* delighted in stillness. When King Aśoka came to make offerings:

> "Aśoka spoke a verse: 'Although he trained himself in removing ignorance, he was of little benefit to the world.' He offered twenty cowrie shells."

But when Aśoka offered a single coin, it flew out of the stupa and attached itself to his foot. Even dead, Vakkula was rejecting unnecessary offerings. (*Ekottara*: "Even his stupa had such power of leisure, stillness, and few desires.")

### The Four Teachings

| Teaching | Interpretation of Stillness |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Tripiṭaka | Extinguishing noise to enter the Real — Tripiṭaka Stillness |
| Shared | Noise *is* the Real — Shared Stillness |
| Distinct | Leaving the two extremes to enter the Middle — Distinct Stillness |
| Perfect | The extremes *are* the Middle — Perfect Stillness |

### Fundamental and Trace

> "He abides in the Great Quiescent Samādhi. Long life is **Permanence**; no sickness is **Bliss**; no premature death is **Self**; stillness is **Purity**. Dwelling in the root of these Four Virtues, his trace demonstrates the stillness of the six sense faculties."

Vakkula is walking proof of the Four Virtues. His body, lifespan, and silence all point to the Nirvāṇa-pāramitās.

### Contemplating the Mind

> "The Middle Way of Mind-Nature — identical to Emptiness, Provisionality, and the Middle — is the contemplation of Permanence, Bliss, Self, and Purity."

The silent one teaches through his form. What you contemplate in your own mind, Vakkula demonstrated in his 160-year life.

## Mahākauṣṭhila (摩訶拘絺羅): Big Knee — The Long-Nail Brahmin

### The Name

> "Mahākauṣṭhila (摩訶拘絺羅): Translated as 'Big Knee.' He was Śāriputra's uncle."

### The Origin Story

Mahākauṣṭhila was a master debater. He always beat his sister in arguments. But then his sister got pregnant:

> "When his sister was pregnant, he could not win arguments against her. He knew that the one she carried was wise — if the eloquence borrowed by the unborn child was like this, how much more so after birth?"

The unborn Śāriputra was already feeding his mother philosophical firepower. Mahākauṣṭhila was embarrassed. He left:

> "He abandoned his home and went to South India to read the eighteen classics. Contemporaries ridiculed him: 'These texts are difficult to master over consecutive lifetimes; one lifetime is not enough.' He sighed: 'At home I was defeated by my sister; on the road I am slighted by others.' He vowed to read without resting, having no time to cut his nails."

Hence: **The Long-Nail Brahmin** (*Dīrghanakha*).

### The Confrontation

He finished his studies and came home. He heard Śāriputra — now grown — had become the Buddha's disciple. He went to confront the Buddha directly:

> "He went to the Buddha. He thought for a long time but could not get a single dharma into his mind. He told the Buddha: 'I do not accept all dharmas.' This means: 'I can destroy all dharmas so they cannot find stability.'"

The Buddha replied with a question that collapsed his logic:

> "The Buddha asked: 'Do you accept this accepting?'"

Checkmate. If he says "I accept," he contradicts his claim. If he says "I don't accept," he admits the Buddha has won. He lowered his head and attained the Purity of the Dharma Eye.

Meanwhile:

> "Śāriputra, who was fanning the Buddha and heard his uncle's debate, attained the fruit of Arhatship."

The nephew, listening in, got the final fruit just by overhearing the match.

*Ekottara*:

> "Among those who attain the Four Eloquences and, when confronted with difficulties, are able to answer, Kauṣṭhila is foremost."

### The Four Teachings

| Teaching | Interpretation of Eloquence |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Tripiṭaka | Externally mastering the Four Vedas, internally mastering the Tripiṭaka — Four Eloquences |
| Shared | "I attain nothing," yet eloquence is like this |
| Distinct | Names and meanings pervade the Ten Dharma Realms |
| Perfect | Relying on **Reality Mark**, pervading all eloquence |

### Fundamental and Trace

> "He abides in the Secret of the Mouth, the inconceivable transformation of the Voice Wheel, Great Concentration and Great Wisdom. His trace manifests as 'Big Knee.'"

The Voice Wheel (*vācī-cakra*) — one of the Buddha's three mysteries. Mahākauṣṭhila's fundamental nature is the Buddha's own teaching-power, wearing the costume of a long-nailed scholar.

### Contemplating the Mind

> "Contemplating the mind as identical to Emptiness and Samādhi, identical to Provisionality and Wisdom, thereby adorning the mind."

Concentration and wisdom together. Silence and speech unified. This is the mind that debates and surrenders in the same breath.

---

**The Contrast**:

| Vakkula | Mahākauṣṭhila |
|---------|---------------|
| Never spoke | Won every argument |
| Six senses sealed | Voice Wheel sovereign |
| 160 years of stillness | Four Vedas + Tripiṭaka mastered |
| Rejected the world | Confronted the Buddha |

Both are Arhats. Both are foremost. The studio needs the silent one and the speaker.
