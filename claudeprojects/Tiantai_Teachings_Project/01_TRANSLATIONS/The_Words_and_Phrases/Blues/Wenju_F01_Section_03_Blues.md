# Fascicle 1, Section 3: Survey of Prior Divisions

## The Earlier Critics — Who Sliced the Album Before Zhiyi

**Transformed by Master John "The Bluesman" Kim (The Liner Notes Variant)**
**Date**: February 6, 2026
**Root Metaphor**: THE RECORDING STUDIO
**Source**: T34n1718, Zhiyi's Survey of Prior Commentators

---

Now listen.

Before Zhiyi wrote his liner notes, other critics had taken a knife to the Lotus Sutra. They divided it, sectioned it, imposed schemas on it. Some of them were brilliant. Some of them obscured the Album with so many divisions you couldn't hear the music.

Zhiyi respects them all — orchids and chrysanthemums, he says, each have their own beauty — but he's also clear about where they went wrong. This section is his survey of the tradition, his acknowledgment of the cats who came before, and his quiet correction of their errors.

---

## Part 1: The Original Teaching

First, Zhiyi reminds you that the Buddha didn't teach with chapter titles:

> "The Buddha responded to conditions and gave two kinds of teaching: 'scattered flowers' and 'threaded flowers.' The compilers at the council transmitted the teachings according to the Buddha's discourses; the treatise masters expounded them in accordance with the sutras. In neither case did they impose topical divisions."

"Scattered flowers" — teachings organized topically but not in strict order, like petals dropped on the ground.

"Threaded flowers" — teachings in a linear, progressive sequence, like a garland.

The Buddha used both. He didn't label sections. He didn't say "Track 1, Track 2." The divisions came later — from the compilers, the translators, the commentators.

> "The ancient lecturers merely expounded the doctrinal principles broadly without dividing the text into chapters and sections. If this approach were used exclusively, later students would scarcely recognize where arguments begin and end."

Structure helps. But too much structure kills the music.

---

## Part 2: The Āgama Classification

Before the Mahāyāna sutras, there were the Āgamas — the early collections. Zhiyi notes how they were organized:

> "The *Ekottara Āgama* clarifies causes and effects among humans and devas; the *Dīrgha Āgama* refutes heterodox views; the *Madhyama Āgama* clarifies profound meanings; the *Saṃyukta Āgama* clarifies meditative concentration."

Each Āgama had a specialty. Each was organized by theme. Even the Vinaya (monastic discipline) had five schools. The Abhidharma had the Six Feet and the Eight Aggregates.

The point: organizing texts isn't new. The question is *how* to organize them wisely.

---

## Part 3: Vasubandhu's Division

The treatise-master Vasubandhu (天親, 4th–5th century) wrote a commentary on the Lotus and imposed his own divisions:

> "Vasubandhu composed a treatise in which he divided the 'Introductory' chapter using seven merits, divided the 'Skillful Means' chapter using five manifestations, and divided each of the remaining chapters according to its respective topic."

Seven merits for the Introduction. Five manifestations for Skillful Means. Vasubandhu gave every chapter its own schema.

This was influential. But it wasn't the only approach.

---

## Part 4: The Chinese Commentators

Now comes the Chinese lineage — the critics who worked with Kumārajīva's translation and tried to map the Album:

### Feng of Hexi and Yao of Jiangdong

> "In former times, Feng of Hexi and Yao of Jiangdong adopted this approach and imposed topical divisions on the sutra text. In the degenerate age, such divisions became especially cumbersome."

Early commentators. Good intentions. But the result was cluttered.

### Guangzhai Yun (Fayun, 467–529)

And then there was Fayun — the man who made everything finer and finer until you couldn't see the stars:

> "Guangzhai made them even finer, until they were like layered mists obscuring the Great Clarity — the sun, moon, and stars concealed their radiance, and those who inquired after the ford did not value such divisions."

Fayun at Guangzhai Temple in Nanjing was meticulous. He divided the sutra into twenty-four sections, each subdivided further. General Sequence and Specific Sequence. Cause Gate and Fruit Gate. Two Sequences × five divisions each. Two Main sections × four each. Two Circulations × three each.

Too much. Tanluan, the Pure Land master, called it out:

> "Minute divisions scatter like smoke; miscellaneous analyses fly like dust."

When your schema is denser than the text, you've lost the thread.

---

## Part 5: Master Long (Daosheng) of Lushan

Here's a better model. Daosheng (道生, ca. 360–434) at Mount Lu — one of the most influential early Chinese Buddhist thinkers:

> "Master Long of Lushan divided the text into Sequence, Main, and Circulation. The twenty-seven chapters are unified into just two categories: From 'Introductory' through 'Dharma Teacher' concerns *verbal skillful means* and *verbal reality*... From 'Jeweled Stupa' onward concerns *bodily skillful means* and *bodily reality*..."

Two halves. Verbal and bodily. One is about what the Buddha *says* (Chapters 1–10); the other is about what the Buddha *is* (Chapters 11–28). And within that:

> "From 'Skillful Means' through 'Peaceful Practices' constitutes the Cause Gate; from 'Springing Up from the Earth' onward constitutes the Fruit Gate."

Cause and Fruit. Practice and Realization. This is cleaner. This is closer to how the Album breathes.

---

## Part 6: Other Divisions

Zhiyi surveys a few more:

**Xuanchang's Division:**

- From "Introductory" through "Abundant Treasures" = Cause Section
- From "Exhortation to Maintain" through "Spiritual Powers" = Fruit Section
- From "Entrustment" through the end = Protection and Maintenance Section

**Anonymous Master's Threefold Division:**

- From "Introductory" through "Prophecy for Those in Training and Beyond" = the *essence* of the Lotus
- From "Dharma Teacher" through "Entrustment" = the merit of receiving and maintaining
- From "Medicine King" through the end = praise of bodhisattvas' original vows

**Anonymous Master's Fourfold Division:**

- First chapter = Sequence
- "Skillful Means" through "Peaceful Practices" = Opening the Three, Revealing the One
- "Springing Up" through "Distinction of Merits" = Opening the Near, Revealing the Remote
- Remainder = Circulation

Each has merits. Each has limits.

---

## Part 7: Zhiyi's Judgment

Zhiyi doesn't tear them down. He says:

> "All divisions and sections of the sutra text are products of human interpretation. Orchids and chrysanthemums each excel in their own beauty. Later students should not engage in disputes over right and wrong, lest they forfeit the three benefits and lose the One Path."

But then he gives his own:

**The Threefold Division:**

1. **Sequence** (序) — Chapter 1
2. **Main** (正) — Chapters 2 through nineteen lines of verse in Chapter 17 (15.5 chapters)
3. **Circulation** (流通) — Remainder of Chapter 17 through Chapter 28 (11.5 chapters)

**The Twofold Division (Trace and Fundamental):**

1. **Trace Gate** (迹門) — Chapters 1–14: Opening the provisional and revealing the real *with regard to the Trace* (the historical Buddha)
2. **Fundamental Gate** (本門) — Chapters 15–28: Opening the provisional and revealing the real *with regard to the Fundamental* (the eternal Buddha)

Each gate has its own Sequence, Main, and Circulation. Two halves, each complete.

This is Zhiyi's schema. This is how he listens to the Album.

---

**The Lesson**: Before Zhiyi, the critics sliced the Lotus a dozen ways. Some divisions were too sparse; some were too fine. Zhiyi honors them all — and then gives you the cut that works. Threefold: Sequence, Main, Circulation. Twofold: Trace and Fundamental. Side A and Side B. Know the earlier reviewers, but listen with Zhiyi's ear.
