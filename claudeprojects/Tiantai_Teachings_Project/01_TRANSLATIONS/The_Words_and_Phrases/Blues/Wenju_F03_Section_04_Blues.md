### Asking About Offerings to Relics (Sixth Part: Seven Lines)

From "Manjushri, there are also Bodhisattvas" onward — seven lines — Maitreya asks about bodhisattvas raising stupas for relics after the Buddha's extinction in those other lands.

Now remember: the old critics said Maitreya doesn't ask about the Buddha's nirvana. Zhiyi already dismantled that. But here's where the proof lands. This section — asking about stupa-building after the Buddha's death — *is* the nirvana question. You build a stupa because somebody's gone. Asking about stupas is asking about extinction.

And what does the stupa-building symbolize? Zhiyi says: "Immeasurable returning entering One, and One producing Immeasurable." All the different teachings, all the varied practices — they all flow back into the One Vehicle. And that One Vehicle pours back out into immeasurable forms. It's circulation. It's the music coming back around.

Then Zhiyi turns to the old critics one more time, and this passage has real fire:

"The previous omens already symbolized 'Immeasurable returning to One,' which *is* entering Nirvana. Why fear that this obstructs the Eternal Life Span, and thus avoid treating 'Raising Stupas' as a Buddha Work? **Painful! Painful!**" (痛哉！痛哉！)

"Painful! Painful!" — that's Zhiyi losing his cool. The Liner Notes Writer, who's usually measured and precise, just dropped his pen and said, "This hurts me." The old critics were so afraid that mentioning nirvana would contradict the Lifespan chapter (Track 16 on Side B, where the Buddha reveals he's eternal) that they refused to see what was right in front of them. Zhiyi says: *not seeing* is what's painful.

Now Zhiyi breaks down the stupa section into six structural parts:

1. **One line** — Generally marks "Buddha's extinction and raising a stupa"
2. **One line** — Clarifies the number of stupas
3. **One line** — Clarifies the size and measure
4. **One line** — Clarifies the stupa-forms
5. **One line** — Clarifies the offerings
6. **Two lines** — Concluding

And a beautiful etymological note: "*Stupa*" (塔婆) is translated as "Square Tomb" (方墳). A Square Tomb is like a grave mound in China. Palace Halls are like spirit-shrines — "lofty terraces and steep steps receiving dew and drying clouds, long symbolizing the Pure Limit and returning the heart to the Superior Sage."

The "Tree King" is the **Parijata** — the celestial Coral Tree growing in the heaven of the Thirty-Three. Strictly speaking, it offers to the Relics; incidentally, it adorns the Buddha Land. Even the ornament is an offering. Even the decoration is devotion.

### The Request for Answer (Eight Lines)

From "The Buddha emits light" onward — eight lines — Maitreya turns to Manjushri and says: *Answer me*.

This splits in two:

1. **First three lines** — Raise the doubt and state the request
2. **Latter five lines** — Explain the **Latent Difficulties** (伏難)

The first three lines have three parts of their own:

1. **One line** — Raises seeing This Land's matters (the White Hair-Mark is the root, so it's raised first)
2. **One line** — Raises seeing Other Lands' matters (the Buddhas are the root, encompassing the other five omens)
3. **One line** — Asks for the answer: "We and others — tell us"

---

### The Four Latent Difficulties: A Master Class in Call-and-Response

Now comes one of the most brilliant passages in the entire Wenju. The **Four Latent Difficulties** (四伏難) — where Manjushri *pretends he can't answer* and Maitreya *forces him to*.

Zhiyi explains: "Latent Difficulties" means Manjushri internally constructs difficulties and is unwilling to answer immediately. His intent has three points:

1. **This omen is rare and strange** — one cannot hastily judge it lightly
2. **The wise assembly is like an ocean** — he modestly defers to the Buddha
3. **Holding back to generate thirst and admiration** — making the assembly want the answer even more

That's Manjushri's game. He's not really stumped. He's an ancient Buddha — of course he knows. But he's creating *tension*. He's the elder musician who sits back, doesn't rush to the mic, lets the audience lean in. The longer you wait, the deeper the listening.

And Maitreya? He's explicit, burning. His intent also has three points:

1. **The omen is great, the doubt is great** — if nobody explains it, the "Worried Rhinoceros" (憂兕) sitting in the chest will obstruct hearing the true teaching
2. **Though the Ocean Assembly is many, the Catalyst is in the Benevolent One** — Manjushri is the one who can do this
3. **The whole assembly looks to the Benevolent One** — their sincerity is heavy

So they go back and forth. Four rounds. Listen:

---

**Difficulty 1**: Maitreya asks Manjushri to "resolve the assembly's doubts." Manjushri fires back: *"You say the assembly doubts? The assembly has not doubted. If they doubted, they would ask. Since they don't doubt, what is there for me to resolve?"*

Maitreya answers with the first verse: "The Four Assemblies look up to the Benevolent One *and me*." "And me" means they want me to ask. "Look up to Benevolent One" means they want you to answer. *We're the call-and-response, you and me. They're waiting.*

---

**Difficulty 2**: Manjushri says: *"If the assembly collectively doubts, it is not easy to answer. Wait for the Buddha to exit samadhi. Then resolve the doubt."*

Maitreya answers with the second verse: "If doubt exists, holding the 'Worried Rhinoceros' is not peaceful. You should answer in time. Who knows when the Tathagata will exit samadhi?" *We can't wait. The doubt is eating at them right now.*

---

**Difficulty 3**: Manjushri says: *"I and the Benevolent One coexist in the Stage of Learning. We can consult together to fathom the Buddha's intent. To make me alone answer is unreasonable."*

Maitreya answers with the third verse: "I also have a subtle mind and ponder, hesitating between two pillars: Is it to expound Wondrous Dharma? Or is it to bestow Prediction?" *I'm guessing too, Manjushri. I can't be the one. You have to be.*

---

**Difficulty 4**: Manjushri says: *"If it is as you say — prediction or preaching — that is explaining the doubt. Why bother me to answer?"*

Maitreya answers with the fourth verse: "How can my hesitant mind judge the Great Matter? Manifesting Buddha Lands — *this is not a small condition*." *My guess isn't good enough. This is too big for guesses. You have to give us the real answer.*

---

Manjushri's latent difficulties are exhausted. His modesty stops. The last verse concludes the Request to Answer.

And Zhiyi adds a note on lineage: these four latent difficulties were transmitted from **Master Zhao of Jiangbei** to **Master Ci** to **Guangzhai (Fayun)**. "Since they are earlier worthies' ingenious reflections outside the text, we adopt them here."

Even the Liner Notes Writer, who corrects Guangzhai on everything else, gives credit where it's due. The Four Latent Difficulties were a good idea. Even a critic who gets the big picture wrong can nail a beautiful detail.

### The Lesson

Before Manjushri gives his answer, he makes Maitreya *earn* the question. Four times he pushes back — "Are they really doubting?" "Can't we wait?" "Why just me?" "Haven't you already answered it yourself?" And four times Maitreya pushes harder — "They're looking at us." "The doubt is eating them alive." "I can't do this alone." "This is too big for guesses." It's the most human moment in the whole Introduction — two ancient beings, both playing their roles perfectly, creating the tension that makes the answer matter. When somebody finally tells you the truth, it lands harder if you had to fight for it.
