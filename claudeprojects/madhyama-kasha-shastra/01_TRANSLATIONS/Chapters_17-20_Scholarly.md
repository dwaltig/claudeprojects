# Chapters 17-20: Scholarly Translations

## Chapter 17: Karmaphala-parīkṣā (Examination of Action and Fruit)

### Introduction
This long chapter (33 verses) addresses a central Buddhist concern: how can karma (action) produce its fruit (*phala*) across lifetimes if there is no permanent self (*ātman*) to bear the karmic residue? Nāgārjuna uses the analogy of a seed to show that karmic continuity occurs through dependent origination, not through an inherently existent agent or bearer.

### Key Verses Summarized
- **Verses 1-6:** Action that clings (*cetanā*) is described as a predisposition that ceases after planting the karmic "seed." The relationship between deed and doer is established as conventional, not substantial.
- **Verses 7-12:** The "seed" analogy: just as a seed produces a plant without the seed persisting, so action produces fruit without an enduring agent. The *ālayavijñāna* (storehouse consciousness) of later Yogācāra is anticipated here as a similar explanatory strategy.
- **Verses 20-33:** The standard objections (If no self, who acts? Who experiences the result?) are addressed. Karma operates through dependent designation (*prajñapti*), not through substantial causation.

### Core Argument
- Karma does not require an eternal self to function; the causal series is maintained through conditions, not substances.
- The "seed" model shows that results can arise from causes that no longer exist—just as the plant is not the seed.
- This preserves ethical responsibility conventionally while denying ultimate selfhood.

### Key Terms

| Sanskrit | English | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Karma** | Action, deed | Volitional action that conditions future experience |
| **Phala** | Fruit, result | The karmic consequence |
| **Bīja** | Seed | Analogy for the karmic potential |
| **Vāsanā** | Habit-energy, impression | The residue that carries forward |
| **Cetanā** | Volition, intention | What makes action morally significant |

## Chapter 18: Ātma-parīkṣā (Examination of Self)

### THE SELF CHAPTER

### Introduction
This is the philosophical heart of the *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*'s implications for personal identity. Nāgārjuna directly addresses and negates the *ātman* (self) as an inherently existent entity. He shows that no substantial self can be found either identical to or different from the psychophysical aggregates (*skandha*). This chapter grounds the Buddhist soteriological project: the cessation of suffering is the cessation of the illusion of self.

### Key Verses Summarized
- **Verse 1:** "If the self were the aggregates, it would be subject to arising and ceasing. If it were different from the aggregates, it would have no characteristics of a composite thing."
- **Verse 6:** Neither is the self the same as the aggregates nor different. Neither is it the possessor of aggregates. It is established through mutual dependence.
- **Verse 7:** "When the Buddhas teach 'self' or teach 'no-self,' they are teaching in accordance with the two truths."
- **Verse 12:** "The self is pacified, like the cessation of conceptual proliferation (*prapañca*). No teaching of any dharma whatsoever has been taught by any Buddha to anyone."

### Core Argument
1. **Neither Identity nor Difference:** The self cannot be identical to the aggregates (then it would be impermanent) nor different (then it would be unrelated to experience).
2. **Two Truths Framework:** The teaching of "self" and "no-self" are both conventional skillful means, appropriate to different levels of understanding. Ultimately, there is neither self nor non-self—just emptiness.
3. **Soteriological Conclusion:** When conceptual proliferation (*prapañca*) ceases—when we stop fabricating "self" and "non-self"—suffering (*duḥkha*) ceases. This is *nirvāṇa*.

### Key Terms

| Sanskrit | English | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Ātman** | Self, soul | The presumed inherent subject of experience |
| **Anātman** | No-self | The denial of inherent selfhood |
| **Skandha** | Aggregate | The five constituents of conventional personhood |
| **Prapañca** | Conceptual proliferation | The mental fabrication that generates suffering |
| **Satyadvaya** | Two Truths | Conventional and ultimate perspectives |
| **Nirvāṇa** | Extinction | The cessation of suffering through cessation of reification |

### Dr. Patel's Note on Chapter 18
> "This chapter is not merely theoretical. It is Nāgārjuna's answer to the existential question: *Who am I?* His answer: the question itself is based on a misapprehension. When the question dissolves, so does suffering."

## Chapter 19: Kāla-parīkṣā (Examination of Time)

### Introduction
In this brief chapter (6 verses), Nāgārjuna analyzes time (*kāla*)—specifically the three times: past, present, and future. He demonstrates that none of these exists with inherent nature, as each is defined only in relation to the others. Time, like all phenomena, is dependently designated.

### Key Verses Summarized
- **Verse 1:** If the present and future depend on the past, they would exist *in* the past—which is contradictory.
- **Verse 3:** If they could exist apart from each other, they would be unrelated and thus incapable of forming a temporal sequence.
- **Verse 6:** The analysis of time parallels that of the three marks (arising, abiding, ceasing) in Chapter 7.

### Core Argument
- Past, present, and future are mutually defining—none has independent existence.
- Time is not a container in which events occur; it is a conceptual designation (*prajñapti*) applied to the flow of conditioned phenomena.
- This undermines any essentialist view of time as a real substrate of experience.

### Key Terms

| Sanskrit | English | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Kāla** | Time | The temporal framework: past, present, future |
| **Atīta** | Past | What has gone by |
| **Pratyutpanna** | Present | What is presently occurring |
| **Anāgata** | Future | What is yet to come |

## Chapter 20: Sāmagrī-parīkṣā (Examination of Totality)

### Introduction
This chapter (24 verses) examines whether a "totality" (*sāmagrī*) of causes and conditions can be established as an inherently existent whole that produces effects. The conclusion: the aggregate of causes is itself dependently arisen and cannot be a single, unified, substantially real thing.

### Key Verses Summarized
- **Verses 1-3:** If the effect already exists in the totality of causes, it is redundant; if it doesn't exist there, anything could arise from anything.
- **Verses 19-22:** The totality is dependent on its individual causes, which are themselves dependent. This leads to infinite regress if one seeks an inherently existent causal nexus.
- **Verse 24:** Arising, abiding, and ceasing are like a dream, an illusion, or a mirage—dependently apparent yet ultimately empty.

### Core Argument
- A "totality" of causes is not a coherent, independently existent whole—it is merely a conceptual collection of individually dependently arisen factors.
- The search for a substantially real causal aggregate leads to logical fallacies (redundancy or chaos) and infinite regress.
- All phenomena, including complex causal networks, are empty of inherent existence and conventionally real only.

### Key Terms

| Sanskrit | English | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Sāmagrī** | Totality, assemblage | The complete collection of causes |
| **Kāraṇa** | Cause | An individual causal factor |
| **Saṃbhava** | Arising, production | The event of an effect coming into being |
| **Gandharvanagara** | City of Gandharvas | A simile for illusion-like appearance |
