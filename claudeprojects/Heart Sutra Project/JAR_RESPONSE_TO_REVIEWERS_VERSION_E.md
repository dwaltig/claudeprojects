## Response to Reviewer 1

**Reviewer 1** noted the "innovative" nature of the argument but correctly identified a lack of engagement with the broader field of Black Buddhist writing.

*   **Revision:** I have significantly expanded the bibliography and engagement with Black Buddhist scholarship. The revised manuscript now explicitly integrates:
    *   **Kamilah Majied** (*Joyfully Just*): Used to frame the "joyfully just" navigation of impermanence (Section 6).
    *   **Rima Vesely-Flad** (*Black Buddhists and the Black Radical Tradition*): Cited to support the critique of "spiritual bypassing" in white convert spaces (Section 6).
    *   **Zenju Earthlyn Manuel** (*The Way of Tenderness*): Her work now frames the discussion of "embodied emptiness" (Sections 1 & 6).
    *   **Ralph H. Craig III** (*Dancing in My Dreams*): His concept of "sonic liturgy" is now central to my reading of the Blues as ritual technology (Section 4).
    *   **Pamela Ayo Yetunde** (*Dearly Beloved*): Cited to reinforce the connection between musical performance and spiritual resilience (Section 4).

## Response to Reviewer 2

**Reviewer 2** offered a substantial and necessary critique of the manuscript's philological precision, source usage, and interpretive framing. I have addressed these "fatal flaws" directly.

### 1. The "Spurious Source" Issue (Self-Citation)
**Critique:** The reviewer rightly flagged the use of my own "Heart Sutra Reborn" as a problematic source, noting it lacks hermeneutical authority.
*   **Revision:** I have removed all treatment of the vernacular translation as an authoritative "source."
*   **Correction:** In **Section 1** and **Footnote 18**, I now frame the vernacular text explicitly as an **"experimental process"** and a "heuristic device" used by the author to test the resilience of the Sūtra's logic in a new idiom. It is no longer cited as a primary text but presented as the *object* of the inquiry.

### 2. Philological Errors & Sanskrit Precision
**Critique:** The reviewer identified errors regarding *ślokas*, the term *mahā-vyavadāna*, and the text's history.
*   **Revision:**
    *   **Removed:** The incorrect reference to *ślokas* (prose vs. verse) has been corrected.
    *   **Removed:** The term *mahā-vyavadāna* (and its incorrect etymology) has been excised entirely.
    *   **Added:** **Section 2 ("The Heart Sūtra as Crisis Text")** now engages directly with **Jan Nattier’s** apocryphon theory and **Donald Lopez’s** commentary history to ground the text's provenance. I explicitly adopt Nattier’s theory of the text as a "Chinese digest," which methodologically supports my reading of it as a site of "creative bricolage" (Section 8).

### 3. The "Ritual Background Noise" Orientalism
**Critique:** The reviewer correctly flagged the phrase "ritual background noise" as "thinly veiled orientalism."
*   **Revision:** I have **deleted this phrase and the entire paragraph**. I accept this critique unequivocally. The revised manuscript acknowledges the rich and active commentarial tradition in East Asia (citing McRae and others) rather than dismissing it.

### 4. Madhyamaka & Ontology
**Critique:** The reviewer noted a lack of engagement with Madhyamaka philosophy and the ontological status of *śūnyatā*.
*   **Revision:** I have added a new subsection: **"The Madhyamaka Anchor" (Section 4)**.
    *   This section explicitly cites **Nāgārjuna’s *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā*** (specifically the "Two Truths" doctrine in Chapter 24) to frame the Blues/Sūtra connection.
    *   I define the mechanism of "survival" not as vague mysticism, but as the rigorous identification of the **"Object of Negation" (*dgag bya*)**: specifically, the *reification* of the racialized self.

### 5. Positionality & The "White Savant" Trap
**Critique:** The reviewer asked, "Does William Altig identify as Black?" and questioned the ethics of the comparative project.
*   **Revision:** I have rewritten **Section 1** to be unmistakably clear: *"I approach this as a white practitioner and independent scholar..."*
*   **Theoretical Shift:** I have replaced the poetic language of "being claimed by resonance" with the more rigorous framework of **"Convergent Evolution."** I argue that the Heart Sūtra and the Blues are structurally similar not because of mysterious lineage, but because human communities under "ontological siege" independently evolve similar cognitive technologies to survive.

### 6. The "AI" Distraction
**Critique:** While not explicitly flagged by Reviewer 2, the previous draft's section on AI translation ("The Chinese Room") contributed to the lack of focus and rigor.
*   **Revision:** I have **deleted the entire AI section**. The paper now focuses exclusively on the comparative theology of the Heart Sūtra and the Blues.

---

## Conclusion

The resulting manuscript is shorter, tighter, and philologically safer. It no longer attempts to "validate" a self-published translation but uses that translation experiment as a lens to explore a genuine theoretical question: *How does emptiness function as survival technology?*

I believe these revisions honor the rigor of the *Journal of Africana Religions* while preserving the article's core contribution to the field.
