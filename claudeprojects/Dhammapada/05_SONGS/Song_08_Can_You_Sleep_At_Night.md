## Notes on the Blues

This song taps into the deepest Blues tradition‚Äîthe reckoning. From Robert Johnson's crossroads to Johnny Cash singing to prisoners at Folsom, the Blues has always known that what you do comes back to you. Not as divine punishment, but as the inescapable weight of your own actions.

**Contemporary resonance:**
- White-collar crime and the sleepless nights of the guilty
- Addiction recovery and making amends
- The 3 AM anxiety of unresolved wrongs
- Deathbed regrets and hospice wisdom
- Cancel culture and the exposure of hidden deeds
- The peace of those who live with integrity
- The burden of secrets

The "can you sleep at night" question is universal. Everyone knows what it feels like to have something they can't forget.

## Notes on the Dharma

**Verse 15** uses *socati* (laments, grieves) three times to emphasize the compounding nature of regret. The wicked one laments HERE (*idha*) in this life and AFTER (*pecca*). The term *viha√±√±ati* means to suffer vexation‚Äîthat grinding, restless torment that won't let go.

**Verse 16** uses *modati* (rejoices) and *pamodati* (greatly rejoices) to show the opposite. The meritorious one (*katapu√±√±o*‚Äîliterally "one who has made merit") sees the purity (*visuddhi*) of their own deeds and is at peace.

This isn't external reward or punishment. It's the natural consequence of what you DO reflecting back to you. Your actions become the mirror you can't look away from.
