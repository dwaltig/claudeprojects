## The Teaching

Verses 17-18 take verses 15-16 and turn up the heat. This isn't just sorrow and joy anymore‚Äîthis is *burning* and *bliss*. The teaching is about karmic compounding: your consequences don't stay the same, they GROW.

The evildoer doesn't just regret‚Äîthey *burn*. The word *tappati* means to be scorched, tormented, consumed by inner fire. "I did that evil thing"‚Äîthat thought alone starts the burning. And when the full consequences arrive? *Bhiyyo tappati*‚Äî"burns even more." The fire don't quit. It grows.

But the merit-maker? They *rejoice* (*nandati*). "I did that good thing"‚Äîthat thought lifts them up right now. And when the good destinations unfold? *Bhiyyo nandati*‚Äî"rejoices even more." Joy compounds like interest in the bank.

This is the warning and the promise: whatever you're growing, it's gonna get bigger. Plant poison, harvest a bigger harvest of poison. Plant good, harvest a bigger harvest of good.

## Notes on the Blues

This song takes the "burning" (*tappati*) metaphor and runs with it. Fire is one of the most primal Blues images‚Äîthe fire of passion, of rage, of hell, of purification. The double meaning works: you can burn with guilt or you can burn with purpose. The fire don't quit either way.

**Contemporary resonance:**
- Addiction and the escalating consequences
- The compound interest of good habits vs. bad habits
- Intergenerational trauma (burning passed down)
- The power of small daily choices to compound
- Guilt that grows over time vs. peace that deepens
- "What goes around comes around" made visceral

## Notes on the Dharma

**Verse 17** uses *tappati* (burns, is tormented) three times to show escalation. The phrase *"pƒÅpa·πÅ me kata·πÅ"* ("evil has been done by me") is the internal burning‚Äîconscience, guilt, shame. Then *duggati·πÅ gato* (having gone to a bad destination) brings *bhiyyo tappati* (burns even more)‚Äîexternal consequences intensifying the internal torment.

**Verse 18** mirrors this structure with *nandati* (rejoices). The internal joy of *"pu√±√±a·πÅ me kata·πÅ"* ("good has been done by me") leads to *suggati·πÅ gato* (good destination) and *bhiyyo nandati* (rejoices even more).

The key insight is **compounding**: karma isn't static. Your consequences grow. This is why Buddhism emphasizes starting now, changing course now‚Äîbecause the longer you wait, the more the flames grow.

### Song Essence

### Visual Concept
A figure silhouetted against flames, but the flames transform: on one side, they're destructive red/orange (consuming, painful), on the other side they shift to warm gold/amber (illuminating, guiding). Two paths from one fire.

**Alternative:** A crossroads at night, one path leads into raging flames, the other into warm firelight from a welcoming home. Same fire energy, different destinations.

**Alternative 2:** Hands in flames‚Äîone pair burning, twisted in pain; another pair holding fire like a torch, powerful and purposeful.

### Color Palette
- Deep crimson and orange (destructive fire)
- Warm amber and gold (beneficial fire)
- Dark shadows and blacks (the unknown)
- Cool blue for contrast (peace, the alternative)

### Typography Direction
Bold, slightly weathered font‚Äîsomething that looks like it's been through fire but survived. The title should feel like a warning and a promise.
