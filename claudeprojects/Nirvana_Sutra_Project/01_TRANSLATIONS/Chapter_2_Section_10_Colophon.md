## Scholarly Notes

**Colophon Structure:**

This brief colophon marks the end of the second fascicle (卷) of the Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra. In traditional Chinese Buddhist texts, each fascicle concludes with a simple statement of the sūtra title and volume number.

**Translation Information:**

The full title of this translation, as stated at the beginning of Chapter 2, is:

**大般涅槃經卷第二**  
**北涼天竺三藏曇無讖譯**

"Great *Parinirvāṇa* Sūtra, Volume Two  
Translated by Tripiṭaka Master Dharmakṣema of Northern Liang from India"

**Historical Context:**

- **Translator:** Dharmakṣema (曇無讖, Tánwúchèn, 385-433 CE)
- **Translation Period:** 421-432 CE
- **Location:** Northern Liang kingdom (北涼), in what is now Gansu Province, China
- **Patronage:** King Juqu Mengxun (沮渠蒙遜) of Northern Liang

This translation became the standard Chinese version of the Mahāparinirvāṇa Sūtra and profoundly influenced East Asian Buddhism, particularly the development of Buddha-nature (*tathāgatagarbha*) doctrine.

**Chapter 2 Summary:**

This second fascicle, titled "Longevity Chapter, Part Two" (壽命品第一之二), contains some of the most important doctrinal teachings in the entire Nirvana Sutra:

1. **The Secret Treasury (祕密藏):** The teaching that true *nirvāṇa* consists of three inseparable aspects—Liberation, Dharma-body, and Great Wisdom—like the three components of the Sanskrit letter इ (*i*).

2. **The Four Inversions (四顛倒):** The revolutionary teaching that there are two sets of inversions—ordinary people see impermanence in what is permanent, and practitioners see impermanence in what is permanent (the Dharma-body). The ultimate truth is **Permanence, Bliss, Self, and Purity** (常樂我淨).

3. **True Self vs. False Self:** The Buddha clarifies that the earlier teaching of "no-self" was provisional (*upāya*), designed to counter ego-attachment. The ultimate teaching is that there is a true Self—the Buddha-nature—which is "real, true, permanent, sovereign, refuge, and unchanging."

These teachings became foundational for Mahāyāna Buddhism in China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam.

## Key Terms (Endnotes for Learners)

**Colophon (卷)**  
A colophon is the closing statement of a text, usually just naming the title and volume number. In traditional Buddhist texts, each fascicle (卷, juǎn) or volume ends with a simple colophon like this one. It's like saying, "End of Book Two."

**Dharmakṣema (曇無讖)**  
The translator of this version of the Nirvana Sutra. He was a Buddhist monk from Central Asia (possibly Kashmir or Kucha) who came to China in the early 5th century. He translated this sutra between 421-432 CE under the patronage of the Northern Liang kingdom. His translation became the standard Chinese version and profoundly shaped East Asian Buddhism's understanding of Buddha-nature.

## Lotus Sutra Connections

### The Bluesman's Take

The Nirvana Sutra and the Lotus Sutra are like two sides of the same coin. The Lotus Sutra reveals **the eternal Buddha**. The Nirvana Sutra reveals **the nature of that eternity**: Permanence, Bliss, Self, Purity.

Both sutras teach the same ultimate truth: **The Buddha ain't going nowhere. The Dharma-body is eternal. And you—your true nature, your Buddha-nature—is eternal too.**

The Lotus Sutra says, "The Buddha's been enlightened forever. His *parinirvāṇa* is just theater, just skillful means."

The Nirvana Sutra says, "That eternal Buddha is the three-in-one: Liberation, Dharma-Body, Wisdom. And that's what *nirvāṇa* really is. Not annihilation. Not the end. But the fullness of truth."

Both sutras are pointing at the same moon. Just using different fingers.
