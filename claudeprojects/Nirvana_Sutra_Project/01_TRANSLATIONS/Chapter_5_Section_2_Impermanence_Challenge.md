# Nirvana Sutra - Chapter 5, Section 2: Kāśyapa's Challenge on Impermanence

**Source Text:** Lines 36-48 (Chapter 5, Tathāgata Nature品第四之二)  
**Section Title:** Resolving the Apparent Contradiction on Impermanence  
**Translator:** Dr. Mei-Ling Chen (陳美玲)

## Agent 1: The Professor (Literal Scholarly Translation)

### Kāśyapa's Challenge (Line 36)

**迦葉復言：「我今定知如來、世尊無所祕藏。如佛所說毘伽羅論，謂佛如來常存不變，是義不然。何以故？佛昔說偈：**

- 迦葉復言 (jiāyè fù yán) - Kāśyapa again said
- 我今定知 (wǒ jīn dìng zhī) - I now certainly know
- 如來、世尊 (rúlái, shìzūn) - Tathāgata, World-Honored One
- 無所祕藏 (wú suǒ mì cáng) - has no secret treasury
- 如佛所說 (rú fó suǒ shuō) - as the Buddha has said
- 毘伽羅論 (píjiāluó lùn) - *vyākaraṇa* (advanced teaching)
- 謂佛如來 (wèi fó rúlái) - namely [that] the Buddha Tathāgata
- 常存不變 (cháng cún bù biàn) - permanently exists without change
- 是義不然 (shì yì bùrán) - this meaning is not so
- 何以故 (héyǐgù) - why is this
- 佛昔說偈 (fó xī shuō jì) - the Buddha formerly spoke a verse

"Kāśyapa again said: 'I now certainly know the Tathāgata, World-Honored One, has no secret treasury. [But] as the Buddha has said [in] *vyākaraṇa*, namely [that] the Buddha Tathāgata permanently exists without change—this meaning is not so. Why is this? The Buddha formerly spoke a verse:'"

### The Buddha's Response: Context of the Verse (Line 43)

**佛言：「善男子！我為一切聲聞弟子，教半字故而說是偈。善男子！波斯匿王其母命終，悲號戀慕不能自勝，來至我所，我即問言：『大王！何故悲苦懊惱乃至於此？』王言：『世尊！國大夫人，是日命終，假使有能令我母命還如本者，我當捨國、象馬、七珍及以身命，悉以賞之。』我復語言：『大王！且莫愁惱，憂悲啼哭，一切眾生壽命盡者，名之為死。諸佛緣覺、聲聞弟子，尚捨此身，況復凡夫？』善男子！我為波斯匿王教半字故，而說是偈。我今為諸聲聞弟子說毘伽羅論，謂如來常存，無有變易。若有人言如來無常，云何是人舌不墮落？」**

**Literal translation:**

"The Buddha said: 'Good son! I for all śrāvaka disciples, because [I was] teaching half-letters, spoke this verse. Good son! King Prasenajit's mother's life ended. [He was] grieving, wailing, longing, yearning, unable to overcome himself, [and] came to my place. I immediately asked saying: "Great king! Why [are you] sorrowful, suffering, distressed, troubled to such an extent?" [The] king said: "World-Honored One! The queen this day life ended. If there were someone able to cause my mother's life to return as before, I would abandon [my] kingdom, elephants and horses, seven treasures, as well as [my] body and life, all use to reward him." I again spoke saying: "Great king! For now do not be sorrowful and troubled, worrying, grieving, weeping, crying. All sentient beings [when] lifespan is exhausted, [it is] called death. All Buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, śrāvaka disciples still abandon this body—how much more so ordinary people?" Good son! I for King Prasenajit, because [I was] teaching half-letters, spoke this verse. I now for the śrāvaka disciples speak *vyākaraṇa*, namely the Tathāgata permanently exists, has no change or alteration. If there is a person who says the Tathāgata is impermanent, how [is it that] this person's tongue does not fall out?'"

## Key Terms Breakdown

### The Professor's Philological Analysis

**1. 半字 (bànzì) - Half-Letters**

- **Sanskrit:** Likely *ardha-akṣara* (half-syllables/letters)
- **Literal meaning:** 半 (half) + 字 (letter, character, word)
- **Why this translation:** This metaphor comes from ancient Indian pedagogy. Students first learned basic syllables (half-letters) before advancing to complex grammar (*vyākaraṇa*). The Chinese translators chose 半字 to convey this pedagogical progression—半 (half/incomplete) + 字 (written character/word) captures both the educational metaphor and the idea of provisional teaching.
- **Doctrinal significance:** This is the second time in Chapter 5 we encounter this crucial distinction. The Buddha explicitly identifies "half-letters" with the teaching of **impermanence** given to those not yet ready for the ultimate truth. This is not a falsehood but a pedagogical necessity—like teaching children basic arithmetic before calculus.

**2. 毘伽羅論 (píjiāluó lùn) - Vyākaraṇa**

- **Sanskrit:** *vyākaraṇa* (grammar, analysis, detailed explanation, prediction)
- **Literal meaning:** Transliteration of Sanskrit + 論 (treatise, discourse)
- **Why this translation:** The Chinese translators preserved the Sanskrit term through phonetic transliteration (毘伽羅) and added 論 (discourse/treatise) to indicate it's a body of teaching. This shows the term's technical importance—some concepts are so central they retain their Sanskrit form.
- **Doctrinal significance:** *Vyākaraṇa* has multiple meanings in Buddhism:
  1. **In education:** Advanced grammar and linguistic analysis (the metaphor used here)
  2. **In prophecy:** The Buddha's prediction of future Buddhahood (*vyākaraṇa* given to disciples)
  3. **In teaching:** The ultimate, complete explanation of reality
  
  Here, the Buddha identifies *vyākaraṇa* with the teaching of **permanence**—the Tathāgata's eternal nature. This is the "advanced grammar" of reality, the full truth revealed when students are ready.

**3. 常存不變 (cháng cún bù biàn) - Permanently Exists Without Change**

- **Sanskrit:** Likely *nitya* (permanent, eternal) + *avikāra* (unchanging)
- **Literal meaning:** 常 (constant, eternal) + 存 (exists, abides) + 不 (not) + 變 (change, alter)
- **Why this translation:** This four-character phrase is the climactic revelation of the *vyākaraṇa* teaching. The Chinese translators carefully selected:
  - 常 (cháng) - emphasizes continuity through time
  - 存 (cún) - emphasizes actual existence (not mere concept)
  - 不變 (bù biàn) - emphasizes immutability
  
  Together, they convey the Mahāyāna doctrine of the eternal Dharma-body, directly contradicting the earlier "half-letter" teaching of universal impermanence.
- **Doctrinal significance:** This is the heart of the Nirvana Sutra's revolutionary teaching. The earlier Buddhist teaching emphasized *anitya* (impermanence) to break attachment. But the ultimate truth is that the Tathāgata's true nature—the Dharma-body—is eternal. This is not a contradiction but a pedagogical progression from provisional to ultimate truth.

**4. 波斯匿王 (bōsīnì wáng) - King Prasenajit**

- **Sanskrit:** *Prasenajit* (literally: "leader of the army" or "victorious army")
- **Literal meaning:** Phonetic transliteration of Sanskrit + 王 (king)
- **Why this translation:** King Prasenajit (Pali: *Pasenadi*) was the king of Kosala during the Buddha's lifetime and a devoted lay disciple. The Chinese translators used phonetic approximation (波斯匿) to preserve his name.
- **Doctrinal significance:** The Buddha's use of this specific historical example is pedagogically brilliant. He's showing that:
  1. The teaching of impermanence was given in a specific context (consoling a grieving king)
  2. It was appropriate for that context (breaking attachment to the deceased)
  3. It was "half-letters"—true for its purpose but not the ultimate truth
  
  This validates the earlier teaching while transcending it. The Buddha wasn't lying to King Prasenajit; he was teaching what the king needed to hear at that moment.

## Lotus Sutra Connections

### The Professor's Cross-References

This passage has profound connections to the Lotus Sutra's teaching on skillful means and the eternal Buddha:

**1. The Apparent Contradiction (Lotus Chapter 2 & 16)**

Kāśyapa's challenge mirrors a fundamental tension in Buddhist teaching:
- **Earlier teaching (half-letters):** Everything is impermanent, including the Buddha's body
- **Ultimate teaching (vyākaraṇa):** The Tathāgata is eternal and unchanging

This is the same tension addressed in **Lotus Sutra Chapter 16: "The Lifespan of the Tathāgata"**:

> "Good sons, the Tathāgata sees the true nature of the triple world: it is not born, it does not die; it does not appear, it does not disappear... Since I attained Buddhahood, an extremely long time has passed. My lifespan is an immeasurable number of *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas, and during all that time I have constantly abided here without ever entering extinction."

The Lotus Sutra resolves this by distinguishing:
- **Nirmāṇakāya** (transformation body): The historical Buddha who appears to be born and die
- **Dharmakāya** (Dharma body): The eternal Buddha-nature that never changes

The Nirvana Sutra makes the same distinction. The verse about impermanence refers to the transformation body; the *vyākaraṇa* teaching of permanence refers to the Dharma body.

**2. Skillful Means and Contextual Teaching (Lotus Chapter 2)**

The Buddha's explanation of why he taught impermanence to King Prasenajit perfectly illustrates **upāya** (skillful means) from Lotus Chapter 2:

> "The Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, wish to open the door of Buddha-wisdom to all living beings... They appear in the world for this one great cause alone."

The teaching of impermanence was skillful means:
- **Purpose:** To console the grieving king and break his attachment to his deceased mother
- **Method:** Teaching the universal law of death to put his personal loss in perspective
- **Result:** The king could accept his mother's death and continue his spiritual practice

But this was "half-letters"—appropriate for that moment but not the complete truth. The complete truth (*vyākaraṇa*) is that the Tathāgata is eternal.

**3. The Parable of the Skilled Physician (Lotus Chapter 16)**

The Buddha's teaching to King Prasenajit parallels the **Parable of the Skilled Physician** in Lotus Chapter 16:

A physician's children accidentally drink poison. The father prepares an antidote, but some children, their minds deranged by the poison, refuse to take it. So the father uses skillful means: he pretends to go on a journey and sends word that he has died. Shocked by their father's "death," the children come to their senses, take the antidote, and are cured. The father then returns.

The parallel:
- **The poison:** Attachment to the impermanent (King Prasenajit's attachment to his mother)
- **The antidote:** The teaching of impermanence (breaking attachment)
- **The father's "death":** The teaching that even Buddhas abandon their bodies
- **The father's return:** The revelation that the Tathāgata is actually eternal

In both cases, the Buddha uses a provisional teaching (impermanence, his own "death") to cure beings of attachment, then reveals the ultimate truth (permanence, his eternal life).

**4. The One Buddha Vehicle (Lotus Chapter 2-3)**

The "half-letters vs. *vyākaraṇa*" distinction parallels the Lotus Sutra's teaching on the three vehicles vs. the One Buddha Vehicle:

- **Three vehicles (half-letters):**
  - Śrāvaka vehicle: Emphasizes impermanence, suffering, no-self
  - Pratyekabuddha vehicle: Emphasizes dependent origination
  - Bodhisattva vehicle: Emphasizes compassion and wisdom
  
- **One Buddha Vehicle (vyākaraṇa):**
  - All beings have Buddha-nature
  - The Tathāgata is eternal
  - Nirvāṇa is permanence, bliss, self, purity

The Lotus Sutra (Chapter 2) states:

> "There are not two vehicles, much less three. Except when the Buddha, using the power of skillful means, makes distinctions for the sake of living beings and preaches three vehicles."

The Nirvana Sutra makes the same point: the teaching of impermanence (three vehicles) was skillful means; the teaching of permanence (One Buddha Vehicle) is the ultimate truth.

## Scholarly Notes

**Historical Context:**

King Prasenajit (Pāli: Pasenadi) was the king of Kosala during the Buddha's lifetime and appears frequently in the Pāli Canon as a devoted lay disciple. His mother, Queen Mallikā, was also a Buddhist. The story of the Buddha consoling Prasenajit after his mother's death appears in various forms across Buddhist literature, always emphasizing the teaching of impermanence as consolation for grief.

The Nirvana Sutra's use of this story is sophisticated: it validates the earlier teaching (impermanence) while contextualizing it as provisional. The Buddha wasn't deceiving King Prasenajit; he was teaching what the king needed to hear at that moment in his spiritual development.

**Philosophical Implications:**

This passage addresses a fundamental hermeneutical question: How do we reconcile apparently contradictory teachings in Buddhist scripture?

The Nirvana Sutra's answer:
1. **Context matters:** The teaching of impermanence was given to a grieving king who needed to let go
2. **Audience matters:** "Half-letters" are for those not yet ready for the ultimate truth
3. **Purpose matters:** The goal is liberation, and different teachings serve that goal at different stages

This is not relativism (all teachings are equally true) but pedagogical realism (different teachings are appropriate for different students at different times).

**The Strong Language:**

The Buddha's statement "If there is a person who says the Tathāgata is impermanent, how [is it that] this person's tongue does not fall out?" is remarkably strong. This hyperbolic language serves several purposes:

1. **Emphasizes the gravity of the teaching:** The eternal nature of the Tathāgata is not a minor doctrinal point but the ultimate truth
2. **Marks the shift from provisional to ultimate:** We've moved from "half-letters" to *vyākaraṇa*
3. **Protects against misunderstanding:** Students might think the earlier teaching of impermanence applies to the Tathāgata's true nature

This rhetorical intensity is characteristic of the Nirvana Sutra's style when revealing ultimate truths.

**Textual Variants:**

Some editions have 太夫人 (tài fūrén, "great lady") instead of 大夫人 (dà fūrén, "great lady") for "queen." The meaning is essentially the same—both refer to the queen mother.

Some editions have 彼王 (bǐ wáng, "that king") instead of 波斯匿王 (bōsīnì wáng, "King Prasenajit"). This is a stylistic variation that doesn't affect the meaning.
