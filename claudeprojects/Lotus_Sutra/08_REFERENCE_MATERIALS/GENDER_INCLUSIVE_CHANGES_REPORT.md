## HIGH PRIORITY CHANGES (Applied First)

### 1. Direct Address: "Brothers" → "Brothers and Sisters"

**Instances changed: 3**

**Impact:** Makes direct addresses to the assembly explicitly inclusive

### 8. New Introduction Paragraph Added

**Location:** After line 278 (Reader's Introduction section)  
**Inserted before:** "How to Read This Book"

**Full text:**

> And let me be clear about something: When this sutra talks about "Buddha's children," when it promises Buddhahood to "all beings," it means ALL. The women are in this house too — always have been. Mahāprajāpatī, the nun who raised the Buddha, she's here with six thousand sisters. Yaśodharā's here. The eight-year-old dragon girl who became a Buddha in an instant — she's here. The eight thousand nuns who received their prophecies — they're here. Avalokiteśvara, who hears every cry of suffering in this world, manifests in women's bodies to save beings. Wonderful Sound Bodhisattva appears as women to teach this dharma.
>
> The blues and gospel traditions know this truth: The Spirit falls on whoever's got ears to hear and a heart ready to receive. Mahalia Jackson, Sister Rosetta Tharpe, the mothers and grandmothers who held the church together through slavery and Jim Crow — they carried the sacred word just as true as any preacher ever did. This sutra's the same way. The Dharma don't ask your gender before it sets you free. The burning house is burning for everybody, and the great cart of liberation is coming for everybody. Sons and daughters, brothers and sisters, monks and nuns, every single child of the Buddha — all of us headed for the same home.

**Impact:** 
- Explicitly acknowledges women's presence and centrality in the sutra
- Names key female figures (Mahāprajāpatī, Yaśodharā, dragon girl, nuns, Avalokiteśvara, Wonderful Sound)
- Connects to blues/gospel tradition's honoring of women spiritual leaders
- Affirms universal dharma promise in blues voice

### 9. Translator's Note Addition

**Location:** After line 116 (end of Translator's Note, before signature)

**Full text:**

> A Note on Language: In this translation, I've aimed to honor both the historical reality of who was present (including the often-overlooked women disciples, nuns, and bodhisattvas) and the universal promise of the teaching itself. When the Buddha addresses "brothers and sisters," when the text speaks of "Buddha's children," when it promises liberation to "all beings" — that means all. The blues and gospel traditions have always known that the Spirit don't discriminate. Neither does the Dharma. Where the original text speaks of specific historical men, I've kept that. Where it speaks to the whole assembly, I've tried to let the language reflect what the teaching proclaims: Every single being, no exceptions, is headed for Buddhahood.

**Impact:**
- Provides translator's rationale for inclusive language choices
- Maintains blues voice while explaining methodology
- Clarifies preservation of historical specificity vs. universal address
