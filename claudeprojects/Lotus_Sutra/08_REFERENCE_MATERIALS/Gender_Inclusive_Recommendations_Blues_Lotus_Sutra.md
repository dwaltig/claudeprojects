# Gender-Inclusive Language Recommendations
## Blues Version of the Lotus Sutra - CLEAN2

**Prepared:** November 1, 2025
**Document Purpose:** Moderate gender-inclusive adjustments that preserve character identities, blues vernacular, and spiritual authenticity while acknowledging women's full participation in dharma practice.

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Current Gender Language Patterns

**Quantitative Analysis:**
- Male-gendered terms (son/sons, brother/brothers, man/men, monk/monks, he/his/him): **1,053 occurrences**
- Female-gendered terms (daughter/daughters, sister/sisters, woman/women, nun/nuns, she/her): **235 occurrences**
- Gender-neutral terms (child/children, folks, people, beings, practitioners): **924 occurrences**

**Key Findings:**

1. **Character identities are appropriately male:** Buddha, Śāriputra, Mañjuśrī, Maitreya, etc. — these should remain as-is.

2. **Women ARE already present:** Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī (with 6,000 nuns), Yaśodharā, 8,000 nuns receiving prophecies, and female bodhisattvas like Avalokiteśvara and Wonderful Sound are explicitly mentioned.

3. **Generic language defaults male:** Terms like "sons," "brothers," "monks" are frequently used where the original text is addressing all practitioners or where gender-neutral language would be more inclusive.

4. **Blues vernacular already uses inclusive terms:** "folks," "people," "everybody," "children," "y'all" appear frequently and can be expanded.

5. **The four-fold assembly structure:** The text regularly mentions "monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen" (the traditional four-fold sangha) showing awareness of women's presence, but sometimes collapses to "monks" or "brothers" alone.

## RECOMMENDED ADJUSTMENTS BY PRIORITY

### HIGH PRIORITY: Generic Audience References

These are the most important changes — generic references that exclude women from the dharma community when they were clearly present.

#### 1. "Brothers" when addressing mixed audiences

**Current Pattern:** Direct address using "brothers" alone
**Issues Found:** ~15 instances

**BEFORE (Line 4844):**
> "Listen here, brothers. I'm gonna tell you about something that happened so far back, so long ago, that your mind can't even stretch that far."

**AFTER:**
> "Listen here, brothers and sisters. I'm gonna tell you about something that happened so far back, so long ago, that your mind can't even stretch that far."

**BEFORE (Line 4848):**
> "Now brothers, listen up. That Buddha passed on into final rest so long ago, the time's so vast you can't wrap your head around it."

**AFTER:**
> "Now brothers and sisters, listen up. That Buddha passed on into final rest so long ago, the time's so vast you can't wrap your head around it."

**BEFORE (Line 5121):**
> "And brothers," the Buddha continued, "from the southeast direction, five hundred million billion countries..."

**AFTER:**
> "And brothers and sisters," the Buddha continued, "from the southeast direction, five hundred million billion countries..."

**Blues Precedent:** Gospel and blues traditions have long honored the "brothers and sisters" formulation. Think of every church service that begins "Brothers and sisters in Christ." This is authentic to the tradition.

---

#### 2. "Monks" as catch-all for all practitioners

**Current Pattern:** Using "monks" alone when referring to the entire assembly
**Context:** The text explicitly mentions the four-fold assembly (monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen) but sometimes collapses to "monks"

**BEFORE (Line 1227):**
> "When he spoke these words, right there in the assembly there was monks and nuns, laymen and laywomen—five thousand of them—they got up from their seats..."

**Keep this as-is** — it already includes all four groups.

**BEFORE (Line 1278):**
> "Furthermore, Śāriputra, them monks and nuns who say they already obtained arhatship..."

**Keep this as-is** — already inclusive.

**BEFORE (Line 678):**
> "And then everybody in the assembly—monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, gods, dragons..."

**Keep this as-is** — already inclusive.

**RECOMMENDATION:** When referring to monastic community specifically, consider "monastics" or keep the "monks and nuns" paired phrasing that already appears throughout.

---

#### 3. "Men" as generic for all humans

**Current Pattern:** Using "men" or "man" to refer to all humanity
**Blues Alternative:** "folks," "people," "human beings"

**BEFORE (Line 293):**
> "These weren't no beginners, you understand. These were men who'd walked the long hard road and come out clean."

**AFTER:**
> "These weren't no beginners, you understand. These were folks who'd walked the long hard road and come out clean."

**Note:** In this specific passage, "men" is literally accurate (these were historical male monks), BUT the blues voice would naturally say "folks" for the general category of "practitioners who've achieved liberation."

---

#### 4. "Sons" referring to all spiritual children

**Current Pattern:** "sons" used for all spiritual descendants or divine beings
**Issues Found:** "sons of heaven," "divine sons," "sons of the Buddha"

**BEFORE (Line 317):**
> "Śakra, the king of the thirty-three heavens, he came down with twenty thousand divine sons."

**AFTER:**
> "Śakra, the king of the thirty-three heavens, he came down with twenty thousand divine children."

**BEFORE (same line):**
> "And there was Moon Prince and Universal Fragrance and Jewel Light and the Four Great Kings, each one bringing ten thousand sons of heaven."

**AFTER:**
> "And there was Moon Prince and Universal Fragrance and Jewel Light and the Four Great Kings, each one bringing ten thousand children of heaven."

**BEFORE (Line 317, continued):**
> "And Freedom King and Great Freedom King, they brought thirty thousand. And Brahmā Sahāmpati, the lord of this whole world-system, and Śikhin Brahma and Bright Brahma, they came with twelve thousand divine sons."

**AFTER:**
> "And Freedom King and Great Freedom King, they brought thirty thousand. And Brahmā Sahāmpati, the lord of this whole world-system, and Śikhin Brahma and Bright Brahma, they came with twelve thousand divine children."

**Theological Note:** "Children of heaven" is more accurate anyway — these are divine beings, not necessarily gendered.

#### 5. "Buddha's children" (already excellent!)

**Current Pattern:** The text ALREADY uses "Buddha's children" extensively — this is perfect and should be preserved and emphasized.

**Examples to KEEP AS-IS:**
- Line 421: "If there's Buddha's children there"
- Line 1937: "I'm a true child of the Buddha"
- Line 1965: "We're Buddha's children too"
- Line 3576: "And we're all like Buddha's children"
- Line 3586: "we really BEEN Buddha's children"
- Line 6970: "This is a true child of Buddha"

**KEEP ALL OF THESE.** This language is theologically correct, blues-authentic, and gender-inclusive.

### MEDIUM PRIORITY: Specific Parables and Teachings

#### 6. The Burning House Parable (Chapter 3)

**Context:** The father has "sons" who need rescuing from the burning house.
**Issue:** This is a specific parable with a father and his children.

**Current (Lines 2242-2244):**
> "Now that elder, he got sons—maybe ten, maybe twenty, maybe even thirty sons—and they all inside that house."

**Recommendation: KEEP AS-IS**

**Rationale:** This is a specific narrative parable, not a generic reference. The father-son dynamic is part of the story's structure. However, consider adding a translator's note acknowledging that "the children in this parable represent ALL beings" — explicitly stating the universal application.

**Alternative approach:** Change to "children" throughout the parable:

**REVISED VERSION:**
> "Now that elder, he got children—maybe ten, maybe twenty, maybe even thirty children—and they all inside that house."

This works because:
1. The parable's function (representing all beings) is clearer
2. "Children" is more blues-natural than the formal "sons"
3. It doesn't change the father-child relationship that's central to the teaching

**BEFORE (Line 2244):**
> "'Even though I can get myself out through this burning door safe and sound, my sons—they in there in that fire house...'"

**AFTER:**
> "'Even though I can get myself out through this burning door safe and sound, my children—they in there in that fire house...'"

**BEFORE (Line 2254):**
> "'But this house only got one door, and it's narrow too. My sons are young, ain't got no sense yet.'"

**AFTER:**
> "'But this house only got one door, and it's narrow too. My children are young, ain't got no sense yet.'"

**This revision makes the parable's universal meaning explicit without sacrificing the intimate father-child relationship.**

---

#### 7. The Prodigal Son Parable (Chapter 4)

**Context:** Poor son returns to wealthy father
**Issue:** This specific parable uses "son" — it's about a father-son relationship

**Recommendation: KEEP "son" in the narrative itself**

**Rationale:** This is a specific story that the blues/gospel tradition knows as "the Prodigal Son." The gendered language is part of the received tradition. However, when APPLYING the parable, use inclusive language:

**Example:**
After telling the parable, instead of:
> "This parable teaches every son of the Buddha..."

Use:
> "This parable teaches every child of the Buddha..."

---

#### 8. References to the Fourfold Assembly

**Current Pattern:** The text already uses "monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen" OR "monks, nuns, lay brothers and lay sisters"

**BEFORE (Line 6244):**
> "You see the monks and nuns, the lay brothers and lay sisters?"

**AFTER:**
> "You see the monks and nuns, the brothers and sisters in the world?"

OR keep "lay brothers and lay sisters" — this is already inclusive.

**The text already does this well.** Preserve this pattern.

### LOW PRIORITY: Contextual and Historical References

#### 9. Specific historical disciples

**Context:** References to actual historical male disciples (Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya, Śāriputra, etc.)

**Recommendation: KEEP AS-IS**

These are specific historical persons who were male. However, when discussing them as a GROUP, consider inclusive framing:

**BEFORE (implied context):**
> "These brothers who achieved liberation..."

**AFTER:**
> "These disciples who achieved liberation..." OR "These practitioners who achieved liberation..."

---

#### 10. Arhat references

**BEFORE (Line 111):**
> "'Arhat' becomes a brother who's 'made it through to the other side.'"

**AFTER:**
> "'Arhat' becomes somebody who's 'made it through to the other side.'"

OR

> "'Arhat' becomes one of the faithful who's 'made it through to the other side.'"

**Rationale:** The blues voice can say "somebody" or "one of the faithful" naturally. "Brother" here excludes women arhats (who definitely existed in the tradition).

1. **Historical character identities:** Buddha, Śāriputra, Mañjuśrī, Maitreya, etc. remain male
2. **Specific parable characters:** The prodigal "son" in Chapter 4 (when referring to the character in the story)

## THEOLOGICAL JUSTIFICATION

### From the Lotus Sutra itself:

1. **Chapter 12 (Devadatta):** The eight-year-old dragon GIRL instantly becomes a Buddha, proving that gender is no barrier to enlightenment.

2. **Chapter 13:** The assembly includes "monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen" — all four groups receive teachings.

3. **The Universal Promise:** "All beings without exception will become Buddhas" — this explicitly includes women.

4. **Historical reality:** Mahāprajāpatī led 6,000 nuns. Women were present, practicing, and achieving liberation.

### From the Blues/Gospel Tradition:

1. **The Spirit falls on all flesh:** "Your sons AND your daughters shall prophesy" (Acts 2:17 / Joel 2:28)
2. **No male or female in Christ:** Galatians 3:28
3. **Women's spiritual authority:** Sojourner Truth, Harriet Tubman (called "Moses"), gospel singers, church mothers

**These traditions align perfectly:** Both proclaim universal liberation regardless of gender.

## CONCLUSION

These recommendations aim to honor three commitments simultaneously:

1. **Historical accuracy:** Keep specific male characters as male; acknowledge women already present
2. **Theological truth:** The Dharma's promise is universal — language should reflect that
3. **Cultural authenticity:** Blues and gospel traditions have always honored "brothers AND sisters"

The goal is NOT to erase gender or rewrite history, but to ensure that when the Buddha speaks to "all beings," when the text promises liberation to "everyone," when it addresses the "children of the Buddha" — the language makes clear that women are included, honored, and equally destined for enlightenment.

This is a blues translation. The blues has always known: **The truth is for everybody, and everybody's got a right to hear their name called.**
