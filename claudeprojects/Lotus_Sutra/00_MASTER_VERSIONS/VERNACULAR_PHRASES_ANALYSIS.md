## CANDIDATE #1: "Preacher" / "Preacher Man" ↔ "Dharma-Teacher"

### Current State:
- **"Dharma-teacher"**: 7 instances
- **"preacher" / "preacher man"**: 20 instances
- **Ratio**: 74% vernacular, 26% traditional

### Documentation:
From Chapter 8 Interpretation Notes:
> "2. 'Preacher man' - Pūrṇa's designation as foremost in teaching the Dharma interprets naturally to the African American religious tradition's 'preacher,' a figure of respect and power."

### Context:
- Chapter 10 is titled "HONOR THE WORD-CARRIERS" (about Dharma teachers)
- Pūrṇa (Full Son) is repeatedly called "best preacher man" (Chapter 8)
- Chapter 10 interpretation notes emphasize: "The living carriers of wisdom... In blues/gospel tradition, the preacher, the singer, the testifier... these people are sacred."

### Recommendation:
**MEDIUM PRIORITY** - Strategic weaving at key moments

**Pattern**: "preacher—Dharma-teacher—" or "Dharma-teacher (preacher)"

**Key Locations for Weaving**:
1. Chapter 8: Pūrṇa's prophecy (first major "preacher man" designation)
2. Chapter 10: Opening (chapter explicitly about Dharma-teachers)
3. Verses about teachers/preachers throughout

**Rationale**:
- "Preacher" is deeply rooted in African-American religious tradition
- "Dharma-teacher" is precise Buddhist terminology
- Weaving teaches readers traditional Buddhist vocabulary for spiritual teachers
- Honors both gospel/blues AND Buddhist scholarly traditions

## CANDIDATE #2: "One Great Cart" ↔ "One Buddha Vehicle"

### Current State:
- **"One Buddha Vehicle"**: ~25 instances (formal doctrinal term)
- **"One great cart"**: Used in parable contexts (Chapter 3)
- **Pattern**: Context-dependent (parable vs. doctrine)

### Documentation:
From Burning House parable (Chapter 3):
- Father promises three small carts (sheep, deer, ox)
- Father gives "one great cart" (一大車) to all equally
- Represents the One Buddha Vehicle

### Current Usage:
**ALREADY WELL DIFFERENTIATED**:
- "One great cart" = Narrative/parable language (concrete image)
- "One Buddha Vehicle" = Doctrinal language (abstract teaching)

### Recommendation:
**LOW PRIORITY** - Already functioning well contextually

**Why**: The terms serve different rhetorical purposes:
- "Great cart" makes the parable vivid and concrete
- "Buddha Vehicle" maintains doctrinal precision
- Readers naturally understand the metaphorical connection

**If weaving**: Only at transition moments from parable → doctrine
- Example: "That one great cart—the One Buddha Vehicle—it's what..."

## CANDIDATE #3: "Buddha-Nature" ↔ Vernacular Equivalent?

### Current State:
- **"Buddha-nature"**: Multiple instances (traditional term preserved)
- **Vernacular phrases**: Scattered alternatives exist but not systematized:
  - "who you really are"
  - "what you really are"
  - "true self"
  - "the dignity inside you"

### Key Passages:
From Chapter 20 (Never Looked Down):
> "He sees their Buddha-nature when they cannot see it themselves."
> "Seeing the Buddha-nature in your enemy and declaring it out loud until it becomes true."

From Chapter 8 (Jewel in the Robe):
> "They've been carrying Buddha-nature all along without knowing it."

### Recommendation:
**LOW-MEDIUM PRIORITY** - Potential for strategic vernacular expansion

**Why consider**:
- "Buddha-nature" is abstract for new readers
- Blues/gospel tradition has powerful language for inherent divine dignity
- Could create vernacular bridge: "that Buddha-nature—who you really are—"

**Why hesitate**:
- "Buddha-nature" already works well in blues register
- Term appears in emotionally charged contexts where it's already powerful
- Over-explanation could weaken the poetic force

**If implementing**: Very selective, only at key first mentions
- Example: "seeing that Buddha-nature—that true self, that dignity God put in you—"

## CANDIDATE #4: "The Way" / "The Road" / "The Path" ↔ "Dharma"

### Current State:
**ALREADY ORGANICALLY WOVEN** - No action needed

- "The way" appears frequently as natural blues vernacular
- "The road" appears in verses (blues metaphor)
- "The path" appears as accessible translation
- "Dharma" used when precision needed

### Examples:
- "found the way" (vernacular)
- "walked the road" (blues metaphor)
- "walking the path" (accessible)
- "teach the Dharma" (traditional)

### Recommendation:
**NO ACTION NEEDED** - Already functioning perfectly

**Why**: Natural code-switching between registers feels authentic:
- Blues/gospel: "I found the way," "walked that road"
- Buddhist: "teach the Dharma," "Dharma-rain"
- Transitions feel organic, not forced

## CANDIDATE #5: "Peace" / "Final Peace" ↔ "Nirvāṇa"

### Current State:
- **"nirvāṇa"**: Preserved throughout (with diacritical)
- **"peace" / "final peace"**: Used descriptively around nirvāṇa

### Current Pattern (ALREADY GOOD):
> "that final peace beyond peace, that nirvāṇa"
> "reached nirvāṇa, that's what I did"

### Recommendation:
**NO ACTION NEEDED** - Already well contextualized

**Why**:
- "Nirvāṇa" appears with vernacular explanation naturally
- Term is widely known even outside Buddhism
- Diacritical mark (ā) is preserved, showing respect for Sanskrit
- Blues register already surrounds it with accessible language

## DECISION CRITERIA

**Implement weaving when**:
✅ Vernacular term is clearly documented as intentional translation
✅ Traditional term appears frequently enough to benefit from teaching
✅ Current usage shows imbalance (like 2:108 ratio for skillful means)
✅ Pedagogical bridge would help readers engage other Buddhist texts
✅ Weaving enhances rather than disrupts blues register

**Leave organic when**:
✅ Current usage already context-appropriate
✅ Natural code-switching feels authentic
✅ Terms don't need explicit teaching relationship
✅ Over-explanation would weaken poetic impact
✅ Traditional term already accessible or widely known

## CONCLUSION

The **"Loving Tricks" ↔ "Skillful Means"** weaving was essential because:
1. Upaya is the central concept of the Lotus Sutra
2. The imbalance was severe (2:108)
3. The vernacular phrase was explicitly documented
4. The pedagogical bridge was needed

**"Preacher" ↔ "Dharma-teacher"** is the strongest candidate for similar treatment because:
1. Explicitly documented in interpretation notes
2. Moderate imbalance (7:20)
3. Key chapters focus on this concept
4. Would complete the pedagogical bridge to Buddhist terminology

All other terms are functioning well organically and don't require systematic intervention.
