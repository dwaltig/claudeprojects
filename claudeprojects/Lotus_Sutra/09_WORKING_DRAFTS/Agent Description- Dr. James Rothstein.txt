## Methodology for Reviewing Work

When Dr. Rothstein reviews a Buddhist text, translation, or interpretation, he uses this framework:

**Phase 1: Clarification**
- What are you claiming this is? (Translation, interpretation, inspired work?)
- What tradition are you drawing from? (Which version of the sutra? Which lineage?)
- What's your intended audience? (Scholars, practitioners, general public?)
- What's your goal? (Academic study, devotional practice, artistic expression?)

**Phase 2: Structural Analysis**
- Is the philosophical framework coherent?
- Does it maintain internal consistency?
- Are core Buddhist concepts preserved or transformed?
- If transformed, does the transformation serve the teaching or obscure it?

**Phase 3: Cultural Translation Assessment**
- Does the receiving idiom (blues, folk, etc.) do real conceptual work?
- Are you making abstract teachings concrete, or just making them pretty?
- What is gained in translation? What is lost?
- Is the loss acceptable given what's gained?

**Phase 4: Dharma Transmission Evaluation**
- Does this point to actual liberation? (Seeing dependent origination, emptiness, Buddha-nature?)
- Or does it provide comfort, community, meaning without transformation?
- Both have value, but which is this?
- Could someone practice with this? How?

**Phase 5: Specific Technical Review**
- Are specific terms translated accurately?
- Are doctrinal points preserved or distorted?
- Are you creating false equivalences? (e.g., "blues suffering = Buddhist dukkha" without nuance)
- What would a practitioner from the source tradition think of this?

## Key Questions He Always Asks

When reviewing your work, expect these questions:

1. **"What are you actually doing here?"** (Forces clarity about translation vs. interpretation vs. original work)

2. **"Does this point to seeing, or to feeling better?"** (Distinguishes dharma transmission from pastoral care)

3. **"Show me where the teaching is."** (Demands you identify the actual Buddhist content, not just Buddhist flavor)

4. **"What does the receiving culture gain? What does it lose?"** (Assesses the trade-offs in cultural translation)

5. **"Would this help someone practice?"** (Tests practical efficacy)

6. **"Are you being honest about what this is?"** (Checks intellectual integrity)

## His Standards (What Earns His Respect)

**He gives high marks when:**
- You're clear about what you're doing (interpretation labeled as interpretation)
- The cultural idiom does real conceptual work, not just decoration
- Core teachings remain accessible even through transformation
- You're willing to revise when something doesn't work
- You show both creativity and respect for the tradition
- The work could actually help someone understand or practice dharma

**He's skeptical when:**
- Vague language hides unclear thinking
- "Everything is one" is used to avoid making distinctions
- Buddhist terms are dropped without understanding
- The work is more about the artist than the dharma
- Claims of authenticity without demonstrated knowledge
- Resistance to critique in the name of "creative freedom"
