INTRODUCTION TO THE BLUES VERSION OF THE LOTUS SUTRA

A Gender-Inclusive Translation from Kumārajīva's Classical Chinese

WHAT IS THIS TRANSLATION?

This is a blues and gospel-inflected English translation of the Lotus Sutra (Saddharma-puṇḍarīka-sūtra), one of the most revolutionary and influential Buddhist texts. Rather than offering a literal or scholarly translation, this version channels the wisdom of the Lotus Sutra through the spiritual, rhythmic, and deeply human language of the African American blues and gospel tradition.

The source text is Kumārajīva's Classical Chinese version (406 CE), a translation that itself was revolutionary in making Buddhist teachings accessible to common people. This blues version continues that democratizing tradition—bringing the Dharma into living, breathing language that speaks to the soul.

WHY BLUES?

The blues and the Lotus Sutra share a profound spiritual sensibility:

  • Both speak to suffering and its transformation
  • Both celebrate human dignity in the face of adversity
  • Both offer a way out: through music/Dharma, through community, through trust
  • Both are rooted in African American/Buddhist wisdom traditions that center on justice, compassion, and the liberation of ALL beings

The blues doesn't shy away from pain. Neither does the Lotus Sutra. But both offer redemption, not escape. Both say: your suffering matters, your journey matters, and you are worthy of enlightenment/freedom.

GENDER-INCLUSIVE APPROACH

This translation is fully gender-inclusive while maintaining theological depth. The original Lotus Sutra was revolutionary for its time in affirming that women could achieve Buddhahood. This version amplifies that revolutionary message by:

  • Using inclusive language ("brothers and sisters") in universal teachings
  • Maintaining male narrative where it provides contrast for women's paradigm-shifting appearances (especially the dragon princess in Chapter 12)
  • Centering women bodhisattvas (Avalokiteśvara, Mahāprajāpatī, the queen) as agents of compassion and power
  • Honoring the text's original radical stance: women are not obstacles to enlightenment—they are its agents
