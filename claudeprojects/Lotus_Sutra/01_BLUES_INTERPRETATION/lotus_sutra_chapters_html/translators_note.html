Translator’s Note: The Dharma and the Blues

This translation takes Kumārajīva’s Classical Chinese text of the Lotus Sutra and pours it through the blues—that great river of African American spiritual and musical expression that has always known how to carry deep wisdom in everyday language.

Some might ask: Why?

The answer is that these two great traditions, born worlds apart, share a spiritual DNA.

Both emerge from the raw, unflinching recognition of suffering. The blues is born from slavery, oppression, and the "hard road" of human trouble. Buddhism is born from the recognition of dukkha—the truth that this conditioned world is a "burning house." Both traditions refuse to look away.

And yet, both insist on joy despite the suffering. The blues can laugh through tears. The gospel tradition shouts for joy in the face of despair. The Lotus Sutra promises that even in the burning house, a "great cart" of inconceivable beauty is waiting for us.

This translation is an attempt to honor that shared DNA. The blues has always carried sacred truth in the language of the people. It’s a tradition that understands:

  • The "loving tricks" of a compassionate father (skillful means).
  • The "poverty consciousness" of the prodigal son who forgot his inheritance.
  • The "hidden dignity" of the jewel sewn in the pauper's robe.
  • The "mathematics of grace" where one moment of faith outweighs eons of works.
  • The "prophetic faith" of "Never Looked Down," who keeps declaring "You gonna be a Buddha" even as the stones are flying.

This is not a formal, academic translation. This is a translation for the heart. I kept the famous names—Mañjuśrī, Avalokiteśvara, Śāriputra—and the original 28-chapter structure. But I've let abstract terms become lived experience. "Arhat" becomes a brother who’s "made it through to the other side." "Nirvāṇa" becomes "that final peace beyond peace." "Dhāraṇī" becomes "a power to hold onto the truth no matter how hard the wind blows."

The verses, especially, are rendered to be felt, to be sung. They use the repetition, the rhythm, and the direct address ("Listen now," "you hear me?") of the blues sermon.

Because that’s what this sutra is. It's a revelation, a promise, and a call. It’s the testimony that the world is on fire, and still we sing.

My hope is that this voice allows the Dharma to be heard in a new way, in the key that sang freedom in chains and prophesied justice in the dark. May all beings hear the teaching in the voice that reaches their hearts.

About gender in this translation

You'll notice the text maintains male characters where they're specific—the Buddha, Śāriputra, Mañjuśrī, and the other historical disciples. This is deliberate. Because this sutra's power doesn't come from pretending women were always fully centered in a male-dominated world. It comes from showing women breaking into that narrative, claiming their place, and in doing so, shattering the categories entirely. When that dragon girl becomes a Buddha, it matters because the story up to that point has been mostly men. When Mahāprajāpatī demands recognition, it carries weight because the Buddha had to think about it. I've kept that narrative reality. But in the universal teachings, in the addresses to all practitioners, in the parables that apply to every living being—there I've made sure the language includes everybody. The dharma doesn't have gender restrictions, so the language addressing 'all beings' shouldn't either. This is a translation that lets the Sutra's own revolution speak: first you think this is a man's world, then you discover it's not, and that discovery IS the teaching.

William Altig
Houston, Texas
October 2025
