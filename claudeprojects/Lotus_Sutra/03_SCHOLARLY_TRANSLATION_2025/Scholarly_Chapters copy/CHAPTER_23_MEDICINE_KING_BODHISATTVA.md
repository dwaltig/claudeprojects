# Chapter 23: The Medicine King Bodhisattva's Original Practice
*藥王菩薩本事品第二十三*

## Translation

### Prose Section: The Bodhisattva's Extreme Offering

At that time, Śākyamuni Buddha addressed the Bodhisattva Prabhūtaratna: "Inquire of me regarding the Medicine King Bodhisattva's original practice. How did this bodhisattva cultivate? What dharma does he teach? Pray tell us the history of this one we honor¹."

Buddha replied: "In ages past, beyond measurement in incalculable eons, there existed a Buddha named the Lord of Purity of the Sun and Moon—the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Awakened One, the One Perfectly Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, the Sugata, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassable Guide, the Tamer of the Untamed, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One. He possessed eighty billion great bodhisattvas and an assembly of seventy-two trillion enlightened śrāvakas. That Buddha's lifespan spanned four million billion nayuta eons. The true dharma remained in that realm for a period measured in years equal to the dust-particles of a continent. The semblance dharma likewise persisted through such measure.

"In that realm, there existed a bodhisattva named All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight—a bodhisattva who had long cultivated the roots of virtue, offered reverence to innumerable Buddhas, and perfected profound wisdom. This bodhisattva had achieved numerous samādhis: the samādhi of displaying all forms, the samādhi of dharma-blossoming, the samādhi of pure virtue, the samādhi of celestial play, and countless others².

"At that time, the Buddha Sun-Moon-Pure-Lord taught the Dharma-Blossom Sutra to all sentient beings. All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight heard this teaching and, wishing to demonstrate his understanding, entered the samādhi of displaying all forms. From within this samādhi, he caused himself to manifest in an ocean of amrita (divine nectar) within the void itself. Having perceived one hundred thousand kinds of precious flowers blooming in that ocean, he declared: 'The dharma I now perceive through the Dharma-Blossom Sutra is so profound! I vow to honor the Buddha and this supreme teaching through ultimate offering.'

"Having made this vow, the bodhisattva collected all the flowers from the ocean of amrita and, gathering them together, presented them to Buddha Sun-Moon-Pure-Lord. The vast assembly received this offering and rejoiced greatly³.

"Afterward, the bodhisattva reflected: 'Though I have offered precious flowers, my heart is not yet satisfied. I now vow to offer my very body as a gift of honor to this Supreme Dharma Sutra.'

"The bodhisattva then gathered together all the celestial bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, and beings of the heavens, and spoke: 'All sentient beings, now listen! I vow to make an offering of my own body to the Buddha Sun-Moon-Pure-Lord and to the Dharma-Blossom Sutra!' Having spoken, the bodhisattva began an extreme practice. He ingested all manner of fragrant waters and precious oils—sandalwood, aloeswood, and other aromatics—for one thousand two hundred years. His body became saturated with these precious substances. Then, wrapping himself in silk and jeweled vestments, and anointing himself with expensive oils, the bodhisattva used supernatural power to ignite his own body as an eternal offering.

"His burning body radiated light across eighty billion nayuta realms. All the Buddhas in these realms perceived this offering and spoke these words simultaneously: 'Excellent! Excellent! This virtuous man has accomplished the supreme offering! To offer flowers, fragrances, garlands, or even entire kingdoms and beloved family members to the Buddha cannot equal this supreme gift of one's own body! This is the supreme offering among all offerings, the most exalted offering among all offerings, given in honor of the dharma and Buddha-realization. O virtuous man, through this supreme sacrifice of your body, you have completed the bodhisattva path!'

"After the bodhisattva's body burned for one thousand two hundred years and was finally consumed, he was reborn in that same Buddha-realm in the royal family. His consciousness re-emerged in his mother's womb. At that moment, the bodhisattva addressed his father in verse:

*'Great King, hear now my words—*
*I have wandered in that place,*
*And suddenly obtained the samādhi*
*That manifests all forms.*
*I have cultivated the supreme offering,*
*And I have burned my body,*
*For I seek the incomparable wisdom,*
*The wisdom of the perfectly enlightened Buddha.'*

"Having spoken this verse, the bodhisattva said to his father: 'The Buddha Sun-Moon-Pure-Lord remains present in that realm. I previously made offering to him and heard the Dharma-Blossom Sutra containing eight hundred trillion billion verses. Great King, I now vow to return to that Buddha's presence to honor him again.' With these words, the bodhisattva levitated into the void, ascending seven palm-trees' height, and approached the Buddha's presence where he again paid reverence and made offerings⁴.

"When this All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight Bodhisattva completed his remaining lifespan, he encountered two billion Buddhas, all called the Radiant Suns—Buddhas whose sunlight drove back all darkness—and in each Buddha's teaching he received, maintained, recited, and widely proclaimed the Dharma-Blossom Sutra. Because he taught this dharma to the four categories of disciples, his vision continuously clarified and he continued the practice of bodily offering.

"Subsequently, he encountered another two billion Buddhas, all called the Cloud-Kings-of-Radiant-Liberation, and similarly received their teaching of the Dharma-Blossom Sutra. Through his continued proclamation, he further perfected the vision-clarity.

"Having encountered countless billions of Buddhas, received the Dharma-Blossom Sutra from all, and proclaimed it widely to the four categories of disciples, his complete enlightenment-wisdom was perfected. He thus attained supreme, perfect enlightenment.

"Prabhūtaratna, do you comprehend the meaning of my account? This All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight Bodhisattva—he is none other than the Medicine King Bodhisattva who sits here before us! Such are the bodhisattva's past accomplishments. This Medicine King has offered his own body to countless billions of Buddhas, sacrificing his form as supreme homage to the dharma. This bodhisattva has completed practice across countless eons. These accomplishments are immeasurable⁵.

"Should a virtuous man or woman aspire to perfect enlightenment and yet offer only a single finger or a single toe in homage to the Buddha-stupa—such an offering exceeds all offerings of kingdoms, wives, children, and entire world-systems! Furthermore, if a virtuous person fills the entire three-thousand-great-thousand-world-system with the seven jewels and offers them to Buddhas, bodhisattvas, solitary-buddhas, and arhats—such an offering pales before the merit of simply receiving and maintaining even a single four-line verse of this Dharma-Blossom Sutra!"

### Verse Section: The Comparison of Offerings

*At that time, the Tathāgata wished to restate this meaning through verse:*

This dharma-blossom is supreme,
Most difficult to encounter,
Among all the teachings I have proclaimed,
It stands foremost.

Consider the four oceans with all their waters—
The ocean is foremost among all waters;
This Dharma-Blossom Sutra similarly
Stands foremost among all sutras⁶.

As the Sumeru mountain
Is highest among all mountains,
As the moon is supreme among celestial lights,
As the sun dispels all darkness—
So too this Dharma-Blossom Sutra
Shines most brilliantly among all dharmas.

A Cakravartin king rules all lesser kings,
Śakra rules the thirty-three heavens,
Brahma rules all sentient beings—
So this Dharma-Blossom Sutra is supreme,
The sovereign among all dharmas.

All śrāvakas and solitary-buddhas,
And all bodhisattvas bowing before this,
All those who receive and maintain this sutra
Are themselves supreme among the assembly.

Just as the Buddha is the sovereign of all dharmas,
So this sutra is the sovereign of all scriptures⁷.

This dharma can rescue all sentient beings,
Can liberate all from suffering,
Can provide complete satisfaction,
Like a cool and refreshing pool,
Like fire for the frozen,
Like clothing for the naked,
Like a merchant finding their captain,
Like a child finding its mother,
Like a traveler finding a ship,
Like the sick finding a physician,
Like the lost finding a lamp,
Like the poor finding treasure,
Like the people finding a king⁸.

Thus, if one hears this Dharma-Blossom,
And writes it or causes it to be written,
The merit-fruits are without boundary,
Cannot be measured by Buddha's wisdom itself⁹.

This sutra grants gifts most valuable:
To hear even one verse brings immeasurable merit,
To maintain it brings immeasurable reward,
To proclaim it brings immeasurable blessing¹⁰.

When Medicine King Bodhisattva heard this dharma,
He burned his own body as supreme offering—
Seventy-two thousand years the flames consumed his form,
That countless beings might perceive the teaching's truth.

After such offering,
When his body was entirely consumed,
He was reborn in that blessed realm,
Having sacrificed his flesh to honor dharma.

He encountered Suns-of-Radiant-Light—
Two billion Buddhas bore that name—
And in each Buddha's teaching he proclaimed,
The Dharma-Blossom Sutra widely.

Again he encountered Cloud-Kings-of-Liberation,
Another two billion Buddhas teaching dharma,
Proclaiming ceaselessly this supreme sutra,
Until wisdom's fruits were perfected¹¹.

Having encountered countless billions of Buddhas,
Having proclaimed this dharma endlessly,
Having burned his body countless times,
He attained supreme enlightenment.

This Medicine King's extraordinary power
Comes from maintaining this sutra,
From burning his body in supreme offering,
From proclaiming dharma without ceasing.

If someone wishes enlightenment
And offers but a single finger
To the Buddha's stupa—
Such offering surpasses
The gift of entire world-systems,
The gift of precious kingdoms,
The gift of beloved family members¹².

If one fills the cosmos with jewels,
The seven treasures in abundance,
And offers them to Buddhas
And bodhisattva assemblies,
This vast offering's merit
Cannot approach the simple act
Of receiving and maintaining
Even a single four-line verse
Of this supreme Dharma-Blossom Sutra!

Whoever receives this teaching,
Can expect rapid enlightenment,
Can anticipate Buddhahood's attainment,
Can look forward to dharma-sovereignty.

### Philosophical Implications

Chapter 23 presents enlightenment-practice through its most extreme form: bodily self-immolation as dharma-offering. This raises profound questions about the nature of sacrifice, suffering, and the transformation of physical body into sacred presence.

The bodhisattva's practice unfolds in carefully structured stages. First comes the perception through samādhi (samadhi of displaying all forms). Second comes the flower-offering, which is subsequently recognized as insufficient. Third comes the ultimate sacrifice: the burning of one's own body sustained through aromatic-saturation for centuries.

The comparative logic is essential: even the most elaborate material offerings—kingdoms, treasures, family members—cannot equal the supreme offering of bodily self-immolation. This suggests a metaphysical claim that the body is ultimately the most valuable asset a being possesses. To surrender it is to surrender one's fundamental claim to existence.

Yet paradoxically, the chapter does not treat this sacrifice as termination but as transformation. The body burns for twelve hundred years yet consciousness is not destroyed. The bodhisattva is reborn immediately in that same realm, now embodied as a prince. What burns is not consciousness but form. The text presents this paradox without resolving it: the body can be simultaneously consumed and retained, destroyed and renewed.

The multiplication of Buddha-encounters is significant. This bodhisattva does not encounter one Buddha and achieve enlightenment. Rather, the pattern repeats across billions of Buddha-epochs. Each encounter involves the same practice: receiving the dharma-blossom sutra, proclaiming it, maintaining it. There is no final arrival, no termination of practice. Enlightenment emerges not from a single achievement but from eternal recurrence of the same fundamental practice.

The naming of this bodhisattva is itself instructive. "All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight" later becomes "Medicine King." The transformation from a name emphasizing visible form to one emphasizing healing suggests a spiritual development. The bodhisattva begins focused on perceptual transformation (all beings seeing) and ends focused on healing transformation (medicine-application).

The chapter also articulates a sophisticated economics of merit. Material offering, no matter how elaborate, produces merit limited by scarcity and finiteness. A person can give treasures and kingdoms because they are finite. But self-offering is infinite: the body's burning does not deplete the dharma; rather, each moment of burning constitutes a new offering. Time itself becomes the measure of gift-magnitude.

### Apparatus Summary

**Context**: Chapter 23 marks the transition from cosmic verification (Chapter 21) and eternal assignment (Chapter 22) to practical exemplification. It provides the first detailed narrative of a bodhisattva whose practice embodies the sutra's teachings.

**Central Teaching**: The ultimate dharma-offering is bodily self-immolation sustained across centuries, and this offering surpasses all material generosity. Through such extreme sacrifice, the bodhisattva perfects enlightenment across infinite Buddha-epochs.

**Structural Function**: Chapter 23 establishes the exemplar-narrative pattern that dominates Phase 4. It demonstrates the doctrine that dharma-transmission requires willingness to surrender one's most fundamental possession: the body itself.

**Key Doctrinal Claims**:
- "Bodily offering surpasses all material wealth"—establishes the hierarchy of offerings
- "Enlightenment emerges through repetitive dharma-proclamation across infinite Buddhas"—asserts eternal dharma-transmission pattern
- "The body burns but consciousness continues, reborn immediately"—demonstrates body-consciousness distinction
- "Medicine King represents countless Buddhas' teaching, not individual achievement"—emphasizes collective Buddha-activity

**Philosophical Connections**:
- **Sacrifice and transformation**: The bodhisattva's burning body represents complete sacrifice-transformation
- **Body-consciousness relationship**: The paradox of body-destruction and continuance raises questions about ontological status of form
- **Eternal dharma-transmission**: The pattern of Buddha-encounter-proclamation-reencounter suggests timeless dharma-circulation
- **Economics of merit**: Material offering has limits; dharma-offering multiplies through distribution

**Footnotes**:

¹ **Prabhūtaratna's role**: The eternally-present Buddha Prabhūtaratna, who has been present since Chapter 11, is addressed as witness. This suggests that the revelation of bodhisattva-exemplars occurs under cosmic authentication.

² **Samādhi enumeration**: The text lists bodhisattva-samādhis (meditative absorptions) that represent capacities: displaying forms, dharma-manifestation, purity, celestial play. These are not sequential steps but concurrent perfections.

³ **The amrita ocean**: The manifestation of a precious ocean within the void suggests that samādhi creates virtual realities equal in value to physical manifestations. The ocean's existence depends entirely on meditative perception.

⁴ **The levitation and verse**: The bodhisattva's spontaneous levitation and verse-declaration at rebirth demonstrates that enlightenment-consciousness maintains continuity across bodily death. The new body is immediately aware of previous existence.

⁵ **The past-present collapse**: The revelation that All-Beings-Are-Delighted-by-the-Sight is Medicine King collapses temporal distinction. The ancient bodhisattva is the present witness. Past and present are identity rather than sequence.

⁶ **Water hierarchy**: The comparative framework (oceans foremost among waters, mountains foremost among earthly features, etc.) creates a metaphysical taxonomy where each level has a supreme representative.

⁷ **Sovereignty metaphors**: The enumeration of sovereigns (Cakravartin, Śakra, Brahma) and the statement that the sutra is sovereign to all establishes a political metaphor for spiritual hierarchy.

⁸ **The list of comparable gifts**: The enumeration of gifts (water, fire, clothing, captain, mother, ship, physician, lamp, treasure, king) is poetic rather than logical. Each represents what a being lacks in specific conditions of suffering.

⁹ **Immeasurable merit declaration**: The repeated declaration that merit cannot be measured establishes that some goods exceed all quantification. Dharma-merit enters a different category entirely.

¹⁰ **Triple offering declaration**: The three forms of offering-fruit (hearing, maintaining, proclaiming) suggest that the sutra generates merit through all modes of engagement.

¹¹ **Wisdom-fruit perfection**: The text specifies that "wisdom's fruits were perfected" only after billions of Buddha-epochs, suggesting that enlightenment is not a single event but a cumulative perfection across time.

¹² **Finger-offering supremacy**: The principle that a single finger offered to a stupa surpasses kingdoms explicitly inverts material economics. Sacred offering operates according to different value-scales than material goods.
